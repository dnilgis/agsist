CATTLE ON FEED HITS; BEANS AND WHEAT BREAK LOWER

Juneteenth closes the week with grains selling off into a 3-day weekend, live cattle running hard on a bullish Cattle on Feed print, and gold dropping hard on dollar strength.

🧵 FRIDAY RESOLUTIONWill USDA Crop Progress confirm the Belt caught up to seasonal pace after May's wet start?
⚡Overnight Surprises: Gold DN 4.0% / Silver DN 6.5% / Live Cattle UP 2.3% / Soybeans (nearby) DN 2.2% / Chicago Wheat DN 2.3% / US Dollar Index UP 0.6%

The week that started with a wheat run and an Iran deal ends with a split book: live cattle at $254.80 ran 2.3% on USDA's Cattle on Feed report showing placements down 10% from last year, while soybeans fell to $11.23 and wheat broke to $6.06 as grains sold off into the Juneteenth long weekend with no fund willing to carry risk through three days of silence. That's the story heading into next week. Cattle found a catalyst. Grains found the exit.

🐄CATTLE ON FEED DELIVERSHIGH CONVICTION
📡DRIVERUSDA Cattle on Feed: May placements down 10% year-over-year, marketings also lower.
↺Cattle: COF data confirmed the bullish thesis that had been building all week.
Live cattle at $254.80 had the session's cleanest story: USDA's Cattle on Feed report showed May placements down 10% from last year, and marketings also fell sharply, tightening the forward supply picture enough to push the front month up 2.3%, its biggest single-session gain in several weeks. The ongoing Cargill Fort Morgan/Schuyler plant lockout that began May 19 was already constraining processing capacity at roughly 2% of weekly slaughter, and today's placement data says the supply side of that equation gets tighter before it loosens. Feeders at $366.60 barely moved, off 0.2%, which tells you today's enthusiasm lived in the fed-cattle contract, not in the stocker pipeline. The feeder flat-line isn't a contradiction; it's a lag. If placement tightness holds through summer, feeders follow.
Placements down 10% means fewer market-ready cattle in Q4; live cattle priced it today.
🌾BEANS AND WHEAT SELL OFFMEDIUM CONVICTION
📡DRIVERPre-long-weekend position liquidation; Thursday grain selloff carried into Friday close.
↺Beans: the sub-300K export scenario played out; oil break pulled nearby beans 2.2% lower.
Nearby soybeans fell to $11.23, down 2.2%, and Chicago wheat broke to $6.06, off 2.3%, as traders cleared positions ahead of the Juneteenth long weekend. Yesterday's grain weakness was already a tell; today the selling finished the job. The China re-entry story that briefly lifted beans earlier this week, with buyers reportedly returning after tariff reductions gave U.S. commodities a 20%-off window per commentary from Ag Bull Trading, wasn't enough to offset pre-weekend risk reduction. November beans held better at $11.43, off just 0.4%, which tells you the new-crop demand thesis is still alive even as old-crop got hit. Wheat's move is the one to watch into next week: the 2.3% pullback follows the 4.2% run from Monday's Iran-deal move higher, and what looked like a breakout is now giving back meaningful ground. Corn eased to $4.17, off half a percent, staying in the background.
Old-crop beans took the hit; new-crop Nov held better, new-crop demand thesis still intact.
⚡CRUDE STEADIES, METALS BREAKMEDIUM CONVICTION
📡DRIVERU.S.-Iran peace talks postponed by Switzerland; dollar index up 0.6% pressured metals hard.
WTI crude at $75.76 rose 1.6% on the day after news that U.S.-Iran peace talks were postponed, with Switzerland confirming the delay and sending oil prices back up in early Asian trade. The Iran-Hormuz tensions, with the Strait of Hormuz premium that built since early April now deflating on diplomatic progress, are not fully resolved: five tankers cleared the Strait last week and diplomatic channels reportedly remain open via Swiss intermediaries, but a postponed negotiating session keeps the floor under crude from breaking down further. Crude lost roughly 19% in May as the premium unwound; today's 1.6% bounce is a pause, not a reversal. Gold, meanwhile, had its biggest single-session decline in months, down 4.0% to $4,167, with silver off 6.5% to $64.77 as the dollar index ran to $100.79, up 0.6%. Dollar strength on a risk-on day in equities pulled the metals hard. For ag producers carrying gold as a macro hedge, today's move is a reminder that the dollar-gold relationship still runs both directions.
Crude found a floor on postponed Iran talks; metals gave back a week of gains in a single session.
📊MACRO AND SOYBEAN MEALLOW CONVICTION
📡DRIVERRisk-on equity session; crush complex continued unwinding; biofuels acreage study released.
The S&P 500 at $7,500.58 rose 1.1% on Juneteenth, a holiday session for equity markets that still saw activity, confirming risk-on tone in equities that didn't translate to grains or metals. Soybean meal at $301.30 fell 1.1% and soybean oil at $69.69 fell 1.7%, both continuing the crush complex unwind that started with Wednesday's oil break. The meal/oil spread is the number to watch going into next week: meal is holding better than oil on a percentage basis, which is the opposite of what biofuel policy momentum had priced in for most of spring. A new study out today projecting the U.S. could lose 30 million corn acres by 2050 without new biofuel markets adds long-run context to that oil premium question, but it's a 2050 headline, not a Monday catalyst. Hogs at $95.03 eased 1.6%, Class III milk at $16.07 fell 0.9%, both drifting with no fresh data driver.
Equities ran; grains and crush complex didn't follow. Dollar strength, not optimism, set the tone.
⇄ THE SPREAD TO WATCH
Nearby soybeans / November soybeans carry
$0.20 inverse (nearby below Nov), narrowing
Old-crop nearby beans at $11.23 fell 2.2% today while Nov beans at $11.43 held, dropping only 0.4%. The old-crop discount to new-crop narrowed on the session, which is the opposite of what a strong China buying signal would produce. The spread is telling you the market trusts new-crop demand more than it trusts old-crop demand to show up before August.
📍 BASIS PULSE
Corn basis soft; soybean basis under pressure into long weekend.
Eastern Belt corn basis is holding steady going into the long weekend, with no fresh ethanol or export demand to pull it tighter. Soybean basis is softening as nearby futures took the brunt of today's selloff and merchandisers have no urgency to bid old-crop bushels ahead of three days without news. Western Belt basis remains soft on both commodities, consistent with adequate movement and no logistical pinch. Producers holding old-crop beans in storage are in the weaker position today; the basis isn't compensating for the futures decline.
🧠 THE MORE YOU KNOW
Placements down 10%: the number the Cattle on Feed headline always buries
Today's Cattle on Feed report showed May placements down 10% year-over-year, and that number does more work than the headline feedlot inventory figure. Placements are the pipeline: animals going in today become market-ready cattle in roughly 120-150 days, which puts this cohort arriving in October and November. A 10% year-over-year drop in one month doesn't mean a 10% supply cut, because it's layered on top of prior months' placements, but it moves the needle on Q4 market-ready supply. The cattle market is already at $254.80 near the top of its 52-week range. If the next two months show similar placement trends, the forward supply tightness that live cattle is pricing today isn't speculation; it's arithmetic.

📰 OUTSIDE THE PITNews not moving prices today but in the calculus.
POLICY
30 Million Corn Acres at Risk by 2050 Without Biofuel Expansion
A study from U.S. Farmers and Ranchers in Action projects the U.S. loses roughly 30 million corn acres over the next 25 years if biofuel demand doesn't grow. That's not a 2026 price mover, but it's the structural backdrop behind every RFS negotiation and every soybean oil futures position the funds are carrying right now.
TRADE
Libya Opens First Oil Licensing Round in 17 Years
Libya's National Oil Corporation signed exploration agreements with major oil companies in the country's first licensing round since 2009. Washington is backing the effort to develop Libyan supply as a counterweight to Iranian barrels. More non-OPEC supply coming online over the next few years keeps a ceiling on the crude price recovery story.
WEATHER
Cool Wet Pattern Returns to Heartland Next Week
The 6-to-10-day outlook calls for near- or below-normal temperatures east of the Rockies, with above-normal precipitation across much of the Corn Belt. That pattern keeps soil moisture elevated going into early pollination timing. No heat stress premium is in the corn market yet, and this forecast is one reason it isn't.
