CRUDE RUNS 5.9%; BEANS SPLIT FROM CORN

Trump's Iran blockade reinstatement drives WTI's biggest single-session gain in weeks, while the soybean/corn split sharpens on pollination stress reports.

🧵 TUE UPDATEWill USDA Crop Progress confirm the good-crop thesis that nearby corn is already pricing, or does a condition decline put the weather premium back on the board?
⚡Overnight Surprise: WTI Crude Oil UP 5.9%

Crude oil moved hard Tuesday, up 5.9% to $78.66, after President Trump announced the U.S. would reinstate its blockade on Iran, forcing traders to reprice Hormuz risk that had been steadily deflating since early April. That is the overnight surprise of the week. While crude ran, the grain complex split in a way that matters for 2026 planning: nearby corn ticked up a quarter-cent to $4.41, but December corn fell a cent on the session to $4.63, and field reports out of northeast Missouri and eastern Iowa are flagging dry conditions exactly when corn needs moisture most. Meanwhile, nearby soybeans rose 10.5 cents to $12.07 while November beans eased modestly. The weekly thread question, whether Crop Progress would confirm the good-crop thesis, got complicated today: producers are reporting the extremes firsthand, and Thursday's export sales number will tell you whether the soybean bid has legs.

🛢️Crude Runs on BlockadeHIGH CONVICTION
📡DRIVERTrump reinstates Iran blockade; WTI reprices Hormuz disruption risk.
↺Crude: yesterday's giveback fully reversed; blockade reinstatement changed the math.
Crude oil had its biggest single-session gain in weeks Tuesday, running to $78.66, up 5.9%, after Trump announced the U.S. would reinstate its blockade on Iran. This is a clean catalyst. The Iran-Hormuz tensions, with the Strait of Hormuz premium that had been deflating on diplomatic progress since early April, are back on the board. OPEC also trimmed its 2026 demand growth forecast for the third straight month in its monthly oil market report, but that was overshadowed completely by the policy move. Refiners are sitting in a particularly sharp spot right now: crude just repriced upward while diesel and gasoline were already holding firm, which means crack spreads are widening fast. Producers who have not locked harvest diesel are now pricing this against a higher crude floor than yesterday morning.
Harvest diesel window just got more expensive; act before crack spreads widen further.

🌽Corn Pollination Stress RealMEDIUM CONVICTION
📡DRIVERMissouri and Iowa field reports flag dry conditions during corn pollination.
↺Corn: December fell 4.5 cents; field stress reports add texture the futures board hasn't fully priced.
Nearby corn settled at $4.41, up less than a cent, while December corn fell to $4.63, dropping a full 4.5 cents on the session. The spread between old-crop and new-crop is narrowing from the other direction, new-crop is coming in. Field reports out of northeast Missouri have farmers saying corn needs rain right now, during pollination, and an eastern Iowa grower described conditions this season as the most extreme she has seen in her career, swinging from drought to flood and back. That ground-level stress is not yet showing up in the futures board with conviction, but it is the kind of data point that gets priced fast when Monday's Crop Progress report arrives at 3 PM CT. December at $4.63 is the line: a condition rating that slips below 60% good/excellent reopens the door toward $4.75.
New-crop corn sold off; pollination stress is real but not yet priced at full weather premium.
🎯 Unpriced new-crop corn: hold ahead of Monday Crop Progress. A condition rating decline below 60% good/excellent is your trigger to begin scale sales above $4.70 December.
🫘Soybeans Split From CornMEDIUM CONVICTION
📡DRIVERSoybean oil up 2.1% lifts crush; Missouri field reports support strong new-crop soybean outlook.
↺Beans: nearby/new-crop split sharpened; oil strength doing the work meal is not.
Nearby soybeans ran to $12.07, up 10.5 cents, while November beans eased to $11.94, down 3 cents. The old-crop/new-crop inverse is real and widening. The nearby bid is coming partly from soybean oil, which moved up 2.1% to $71.09, a meaningful session gain that lifted crush economics. Soybean meal gave back 1.2% to $315.60, so the crush margin shift is rotating within the complex toward oil over meal. Missouri field reports say soybeans look as good as ever, set up for strong yields, and that fundamental underpinning keeps new-crop from running with the nearby. The question this week is Thursday's export sales number: above 400K MT confirms the nearby bid has real demand behind it. Below 300K MT, you are looking at a technical bounce that fades.
Old-crop beans bid on oil strength; new-crop flat on good field reports. Thursday exports decide.
🎯 Unpriced new-crop soybeans: hold through Thursday export sales. Above 400K MT, scale toward $12.10 November. Below 300K MT, reassess storage economics.
🐄Cattle Quiet, Data Problem LoudMEDIUM CONVICTION
📡DRIVERUSDA revises beef export data downward by roughly 90%, calling data quality into question.
Live cattle settled at $234.95, off a fraction, and feeders at $354.20, also barely changed. The session was quiet on the surface. What was not quiet: USDA revised its reported beef export sales data downward by roughly 90%, a number that should reset how every participant in this market reads the demand signal. If the baseline export figure was that far off, the supply/demand balance the market has been trading against is suspect. The ongoing Cargill Fort Morgan/Schuyler plant lockout, now into its eighth week with 1,700 workers still out, continues to constrain processing. Add a USDA data revision of this magnitude and the cattle market's footing is shakier than $234.95 lets on.
Cattle held flat but USDA data revision means the demand baseline needs to be rebuilt.

⇄ THE SPREAD TO WATCH
Soybean oil / soybean meal ratio
Oil up 2.1% to $71.09, meal down 1.2% to $315.60; oil's share of crush value rising.
When soybean oil takes this much of the crush margin away from meal in a single session, it tells you the demand driver in the complex has rotated. Biofuel and renewable diesel feedstock demand is doing the work that export-driven meal demand was doing earlier in the season. Crush capacity that was optimized for meal economics is running into a different set of incentives now.
📍 BASIS PULSE
Corn basis softening in West; wheat harvest tightening elevator bids East.
Eastern Belt corn basis is holding relatively firm as ethanol grind continues and summer demand keeps merchandisers active. Western Belt basis is easing, consistent with the seasonal pattern as harvest approaches. Wheat basis is the story with more texture: Michigan and South Dakota are reporting strong early harvest quality, which is pulling elevator bids tighter as they fill contracted positions. Producers with old-crop corn in eastern storage still have a basis advantage the futures board alone does not show.
🧠 THE MORE YOU KNOW
The Refiner's Windfall: Why Crude Running and Diesel Holding Creates a Dangerous Input Gap
Crude just ran to $78.66 in a single session, but diesel at the pump and futures rack prices were already holding firm before today's move. That combination, rising crude plus sticky product prices, is what oil refiners describe as a widening crack spread, and it means refiners are profiting while ag input costs are compounding. For farmers locking harvest diesel, the relevant math is not just where WTI is today but how fast the crack spread passes through to rack prices. Historically, diesel rack prices respond to crude rallies within one to two weeks, sometimes faster when the move is driven by a supply-disruption story like Hormuz rather than a demand increase. Today's 5.9% crude move on the Iran blockade reinstatement is exactly the kind of catalyst that accelerates that pass-through. The window to lock ahead of the spread widening is measured in days, not weeks.

POLICY
FTC Settlement Gives Farmers Right to Repair
A settlement with John Deere now requires the company to give farmers and independent repair shops access to tools and software needed to fix equipment, ending decades of repair lock-in. For producers who have been paying dealer-only rates at the worst possible moment during planting or harvest, this is a structural cost shift that compounds over time.
POLICY
USDA Plan to Aid Small Beef Processors Draws Union Fire
Agriculture Secretary Brooke Rollins announced up to $500 million in aid for small beef processors, drawing criticism from the UFCW over concerns the money will flow to nonunionized plants. The broader issue is packer concentration, and any meaningful shift in processing capacity away from the four major packers changes the slaughter calendar and cash cattle dynamics for every cow-calf and feedlot operator.
RURAL
Oklahoma Poultry Pollution Suit Reaches $41.7M Settlement
Integrators must pay $41.7 million into Oklahoma's state environmental fund and help contract growers install riparian buffers under a new settlement in the state's long-running poultry runoff suit. Contract growers caught in the middle of this one face real capital expenditure requirements on top of already tight margins.
