📊 WEEK IN REVIEW MEDIUM CONVICTION
The week told two stories: grain weakness and livestock divergence. Corn fell 1.8% to close Friday at $4.55 as planting pressure built across the Corn Belt. December corn held steadier, gaining slightly to $4.84, showing the market's focus on old crop liquidation. Soybeans managed better, slipping just 0.8% to $11.64 as meal strength provided support. Chicago wheat joined the grain selloff, dropping 1.8% to $6.08.
Grain prices finally acknowledged planting season reality this week.
🐄 CATTLE SURGE CONTINUES HIGH CONVICTION
Live cattle exploded higher Friday, gaining 2% to reach $248.30 and touching their best levels in nearly a year. Feeder cattle followed suit with a 2.4% jump to $367.62. The strength reflects tight supplies meeting robust demand as grilling season approaches. Meanwhile, hogs crashed 8.8% to $94.30 on seasonal pressure and processing capacity concerns. Class III milk extended this week's decline, falling another 6.5% to $16.84.
Cattle strength contrasts sharply with weakness across other livestock.

🧠 THE MORE YOU KNOW
Why April Grain Weakness Is Actually Healthy
Grain markets typically weaken during planting season, and that's actually bullish for longer-term prices. Lower prices during planting discourage farmers from switching acres to competing crops, preserving planted area for the original crop. This April's corn weakness helps lock in projected corn acres while December futures hold relatively firm. The pattern suggests the market is managing supply correctly, even if it feels painful for farmers holding old crop inventory.
