SOYBEAN OIL BREAKS HARD, EXPORTS ON DECK

A 6.7% drop in soybean oil reshapes the oilseed complex ahead of Thursday's 7:30 AM export sales print.

🧵 THU UPDATEWill USDA Crop Progress confirm the Belt caught up to seasonal pace after May's wet start?
⚡Overnight Surprises: Soybean Oil DN 6.7% / Gold DN 1.8% / Silver DN 2.9% / US Dollar Index UP 0.6%

Soybean oil fell hard Thursday, dropping 6.7% to $66.08, the biggest single-session move in the complex in weeks, and it dragged the whole oilseed story with it. Soybeans eased to $11.41 on both nearby and November, meal gave back half a percent to $303.10, and the setup now sits squarely on this morning's USDA Weekly Export Sales at 7:30 AM CT. Wednesday's announced sale of 13.7 million bushels to unknown destinations gave beans a reason to run yesterday; today's export sales number either validates that buyer or leaves the chart holding the bag. The thread this week has been about whether the Belt caught up to seasonal pace: crop condition reports from the field say yes, but export demand is the other half of the price equation, and that answer comes in hours.

🫘SOYBEAN OIL BREAKSHIGH CONVICTION
📡DRIVER60 million barrels of Hormuz crude heading to Asia pressures soybean oil's energy-linkage premium.
↺Beans: yesterday's 13.7M-bushel sale tailwind gave way to soy oil breakdown.
Soybean oil fell 6.7% to $66.08, the day's dominant story and the kind of single-session move that reorganizes the entire oilseed complex. The Iran-Hormuz tensions, with the Strait of Hormuz premium that built since early April now deflating on diplomatic progress, matter here: more than 60 million barrels of crude sitting in the Persian Gulf are preparing to move to Asian markets as the chokepoint reopens, and that crude flow feeds directly into the vegetable oil price competition. When crude stays bid and palm and other competing oils ease, soybean oil loses its energy-linkage premium fast. Soybeans dropped to $11.41 on both nearby and November, a 6.5-cent drag. Meal at $303.10 held better, only a half-percent softer, which tells you the sell pressure was concentrated in the oil share of the crush, not in demand for protein. The 13.7-million-bushel sale to unknown destinations announced Wednesday is the counterweight: whoever bought that beans is not selling the story short. Export sales at 7:30 AM this morning are the next chapter.
Oil share of the crush just repriced lower; meal held. Export sales resolve the bean direction.
🎯 Old-crop beans in storage: watch the 7:30 print before making any cash sales. Under 300K MT in today's export sales and the nearby stays under pressure.
🌽CORN DRIFTS, WAITSLOW CONVICTION
📡DRIVERField reports confirm healthy crop development; no weather threat to price a premium against.
Corn eased less than 2 cents to $4.18 nearby, $4.46 December, and both contracts are doing exactly what the field reports suggest they should: not much. A north-central Iowa farmer interviewed Wednesday described planting as done by early May, herbicide applications wrapping up, and crops looking healthy. That is not the description of a market that needs a weather premium. The 2026 planting season, behind average through May before catching up between rain systems, has now largely normalized, and with pollination still weeks away, there is no urgency on the upside. The export sales print this morning covers corn too; the Belt-on-pace story keeps a lid on any weather bid, but a strong export number would remind the market that demand is still the other variable in the equation.
Corn is coiled inside a narrow range; export demand is the only near-term catalyst.
🐄CATTLE HOLDS, HOGS RUNMEDIUM CONVICTION
📡DRIVERAPHIS administrator named amid screwworm scrutiny; Cargill lockout continues with no resolution announced.
↺Cattle: yesterday's bounce held but didn't extend; thesis intact, momentum flat.
Live cattle barely moved at $249.05, feeders drifted a tenth of a percent to $367.43, and after two days of sharp moves the complex is catching its breath. The ongoing Cargill Fort Morgan/Schuyler plant lockout that began May 19 is still in the background, the 1,700 locked-out workers still processing roughly 6,000 fewer head per day, and USDA's announcement Wednesday of a new APHIS administrator comes as the New World screwworm situation in Texas continues without a comprehensive response plan. Beef Magazine noted this week that political responses alone won't solve screwworm, and leadership gaps at APHIS have left the cattle industry without a clear path. Neither of those issues pushed the needle today. Hogs were the livestock story Thursday, rising 1.6% to $96.55 on no single clean catalyst: looks like fund rotation into a contract that had been lagging the broader livestock recovery.
Cattle taking a breath; screwworm and lockout both still in the price structure.
📉MACRO TURNS DEFENSIVEMEDIUM CONVICTION
📡DRIVERDollar firmed 0.6% as metals and equities pulled back; risk-off ahead of export sales report.
Gold dropped 1.8% to $4,263, silver fell 2.9% to $67.28, and the S&P gave back 1.2% to $7,420.10, all on the same day the US Dollar Index firmed 0.6% to $100.80. That combination, metals and equities lower, dollar higher, is a classic risk-off rotation, and it is not friendly to grain exports priced in dollars. A stronger dollar makes US bushels more expensive for foreign buyers, which is exactly the wrong backdrop for a Thursday export sales report the market is counting on. India's energy import bill reportedly gained sharply year-over-year in May, and that kind of demand pressure for energy in Asia is one reason the macro picture is getting complicated. Watch the dollar: if it holds above $100.50 through next week, the export window the China deal opened gets narrower without a corresponding price concession.
Dollar strength is the quiet headwind behind this morning's export sales number.
⇄ THE SPREAD TO WATCH
Soybean oil / soybean meal share of crush value
Oil share compressed sharply Thursday on the 6.7% drop; meal held relative value.
When oil and meal diverge this sharply in a single session, the crush margin math changes and processors re-evaluate run rates. If oil doesn't recover toward $70 in the next few sessions, crushers have less incentive to run hard, which eventually tightens meal supply and could support meal prices even as bean futures stay soft. Watch whether the oil/meal ratio normalizes or holds the gap.

Western Belt basis is staying soft, consistent with the seasonal pattern and healthy crop development in the Northern Plains. Soybean basis is quiet ahead of the export sales print; the 13.7-million-bushel Wednesday sale hasn't moved the physical market yet, but a strong export number at 7:30 could tighten interior bids by midday.
🧠 THE MORE YOU KNOW
The oil share of the crush: what $66 soybean oil actually means for the farmer.
Today's 6.7% drop in soybean oil to $66.08 matters beyond the futures board because the value of a bushel of soybeans is split at crush between meal and oil, and the ratio between them shifts constantly. When oil's share of crush value compresses, processors earn less per bushel processed, and over time that pressure works backward into the cash bid for beans at the elevator. The current move was driven by the unwinding of the energy linkage premium, competing vegetable oils getting cheaper as Hormuz crude flows resume, rather than a demand breakdown. That distinction matters: a structural demand drop is bearish for months; a premium unwind can stabilize once the new crude price is fully priced in. Producers sitting on old-crop beans should watch whether oil stabilizes above $65 or keeps sliding; the floor for that stabilization is the non-energy equilibrium for global vegetable oil, and nobody has a clean number for that right now.

📰 OUTSIDE THE PITNews not moving prices today but in the calculus.
POLICY
USDA Moving FAS Workforce Out of DC to Kansas City
USDA announced Wednesday it will relocate much of the Foreign Agricultural Service's DC-based staff to Kansas City and Beltsville as part of a broader reorganization. The FAS is the agency that negotiates trade access and tracks export commitments including the China $17 billion deal; disrupting institutional memory and relationships mid-cycle is a real operational risk that won't show up in Friday's futures close.
POLICY
Dairies Get H-2A Clarity, But the Seasonal Catch Stays
DHS and DOL confirmed Wednesday that dairy operations can use the H-2A temporary worker program, but positions must still qualify as temporary or seasonal, a constraint that covers little of the year-round labor dairy actually needs. Dairies across the Upper Midwest and West that were hoping for a broader labor fix are still looking for one.
TRADE
60 Million Barrels Leaving the Gulf as Hormuz Reopens
More than 60 million barrels of crude oil backed up in the Persian Gulf are preparing to move to Asian markets as Strait of Hormuz traffic resumes. That flow will reprice energy in Asia and take more pressure off the crude-linked vegetable oil complex, reinforcing the soybean oil move today and keeping fertilizer input costs lower than they were six weeks ago.
