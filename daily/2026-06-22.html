🛢️CRUDE LEADS THE COMPLEXHIGH CONVICTION
📡DRIVERIran oil exports through Hormuz hit wartime high; U.S. lifted naval blockade; Kuwait resumes Gulf loadings.
WTI crude ran to $77.67, up 3.2%, the session's clearest story and the only overnight surprise worth naming today. The catalyst is specific: Iran's oil exports through Hormuz hit a wartime high after the U.S. lifted the naval blockade outside the chokepoint, and Kuwait tendered Gulf naphtha loadings for the first time in months as regional producers race to move barrels. This is not a geopolitical fear bid. Iran-Hormuz tensions, with the Strait of Hormuz premium that built since early April now deflating on diplomatic progress, are reshaping into a supply-normalization trade: more oil moving, not less, and the price response reflects improved flow expectations rather than disruption risk. Natural gas added 1.8% to $3.27, consistent with the broader energy firmness. For producers watching diesel and fertilizer input costs, the crude move is a one-session read; the structural Permian gas build, up 60% since 2021 per EIA, is the longer-run input cost story.
Crude ran on supply normalization, not fear; those moves have different staying power.
🌽GRAINS CATCH A BREATHMEDIUM CONVICTION
📡DRIVERUSDA Crop Progress due 3 PM CT; soybean seedling disease pressure reported in Midwest and Delta from excessive rain.
Soybeans added 6 cents to $11.48 and wheat firmed 2 cents to $6.16, both modest reflex bids after last week's sustained break. Corn was essentially unchanged at $4.44, off a quarter cent, and that tells you the grain complex doesn't have a new driver yet. It's waiting on one. That driver arrives today at 3 PM CT when USDA releases Crop Progress. The standing situation coming into this report: corn planting reached 42% as of the week ending May 24, and the Belt has been running ahead of the 5-year average despite a wet May start. The question for today's print is how much further planting and crop ratings have advanced through the wet June pattern. Seedling disease pressure from excessive Midwest and Delta rains, flagged by BASF's Mike Probst, is showing up in soybean fields specifically, with Rhizoctonia and Pythium concerns where seed treatments are only partially effective. If the Crop Progress report shows soybean condition ratings softening, the 6-cent bounce in beans today could have more to build on. If ratings hold steady, the bounce is thin.
3 PM CT decides whether today's grain bounce has a story behind it or not.
🎯 Hold old-crop sales until after the 3 PM Crop Progress print; a weak condition rating adds premium the morning session hasn't priced.
🐄CATTLE SLIP, PROCESSING MATH UNCHANGEDMEDIUM CONVICTION
📡DRIVERCargill lockout processing constraint ongoing; Nebraska cash cattle $256, steady-to-firm but not moving futures.
Live cattle eased to $246.75, off about 0.9%, and feeders held nearly flat at $366.92, down a tenth of a percent. The ongoing Cargill Fort Morgan/Schuyler plant lockout that began Tuesday May 19 is still the frame: 1,700 workers locked out, roughly 6,000 head daily removed from processing capacity, and no resolution announced. The split between feeders and live cattle that defined last week's action has partially broke down as live cattle gave back more ground. A few cash cattle deals cleared Nebraska at $256 live last week, about a dollar above the prior weighted average, which is nominally supportive, but the futures board isn't trading that premium today. A beef magazine analysis released this morning pushes back on the narrative that packer concentration is driving consumer beef prices, arguing demand is the primary driver. That's a longer structural argument; the short-run processing constraint from the lockout is what's moving the calendar spread.
Cattle acting like a buyer who's present but not urgent; lockout resolution is the line that resets this.

⇄ THE SPREAD TO WATCH
Soybean Oil / Soybean Meal share of crush value
Soybean oil at $66.59, up 1.0%; soybean meal at $304.10, up 0.3%; oil share moving wider.
Soybean oil outpaced meal by a factor of 3 to 1 today, and that ratio has been quietly shifting all month as biofuel demand props the oil side while meal sits under export pressure. When oil share runs ahead of meal share, it tells you the crush is being driven by renewable diesel demand, not protein demand, which changes who's buying the beans and why. If the oil share keeps widening, the crush margin story for fall beans looks different than the protein-export story that dominated spring.

Eastern Belt corn basis is holding firm as ethanol grind stays active, but the western Belt is running soft with ample old-crop supply still working through the system. Soybean basis is showing selective firming east of the Mississippi ahead of the Crop Progress report; merchandisers near crush facilities are bidding for beans where seedling disease concern is creating origin uncertainty. 🧠 THE MORE YOU KNOW
What soybean oil's 1% gain today is really pricing.
Soybean oil added 1.0% to $66.59 while soybean meal only added 0.3%, a gap that looks small but carries a structural message. Crush facilities produce both products in a fixed ratio, and when the market values one side significantly more than the other, it signals which demand center is driving the crush. Right now, renewable diesel mandates and biofuel blenders are bidding for soybean oil at levels that support crush margins even when meal export demand is soft. That's why the soybean complex can hold up even as protein demand from China's $17 billion annual US ag purchase commitment through 2028 has not yet translated into sustained futures support. The oil-side bid is the floor the market is standing on, not the export ledger. Producers pricing new-crop beans should understand they're selling into a crush margin supported by biofuel policy, and any change to RFS blending mandates hits the oil-side bid first.
