CATTLE HOLDS $254, GRAINS FACE POLLINATION WEEK

Live cattle closed at a 52-week high Friday while beans and wheat broke hard into the holiday; this week's crop conditions and weather pattern decide whether grains find a floor.

Friday's close told two different stories from the same market. Live cattle finished at $254.80, up 2.3% on the session and sitting at 93% of its 52-week range, the Cargill Fort Morgan lockout (ongoing since May 19) removing roughly 2% of weekly slaughter capacity and tightening the supply picture enough to lift the front month hard. Meanwhile, beans dropped 24¾ cents to $11.23 nearby, wheat gave back 14 cents to $6.06, and the Corn Belt is heading into pollination week with a cool, wet pattern forecast across most of the Heartland. Those two things are not in conflict: tight packing capacity props cattle while ample soil moisture keeps weather premium out of corn and beans. The question this week is whether the crop-condition numbers Monday afternoon confirm what the forecast is implying, or deliver a surprise.

🌽GRAINS INTO POLLINATIONMEDIUM CONVICTION
Corn closed at $4.17 nearby and $4.44 December, off half a cent and nearly a third of a cent respectively on Friday. Beans nearby dropped 24¾ cents to $11.23, though November held better at $11.43, off less than 5 cents. Wheat gave back 14 cents to $6.06, its second sharp session in a row. The soybean oil break that started Wednesday, with oil closing Friday at $69.69 down 1.7%, has been pulling crush complex products lower across the board since mid-week. What matters now is the weather pattern walking directly into pollination: Brownfield's forecast shows cool temperatures and above-normal precipitation likely across most of the Heartland through the 6-to-10-day window. That is not a weather market in the making; that is a yield-support pattern. Monday's Crop Progress at 3 PM CT is the first real data point of the week. Corn was at 42% planted as of late May; by now the crop is well established and condition ratings are the number to watch. Below 65% good-to-excellent on corn would be the first signal the cool, wet start left quality issues. Above 70% and the weather premium stays exactly where it is: zero.
No weather premium until the forecast turns. Monday's condition ratings set the tone.
🐄CATTLE AT THE HIGH, CONTEXT REQUIREDMEDIUM CONVICTION
Live cattle finished Friday at $254.80, up 2.3% on the session and at 93% of its 52-week range. That is a strong number. But context is doing a lot of work here. The ongoing Cargill Fort Morgan lockout, with 1,700 workers off the floor since May 19 and roughly 6,000 head daily of processing capacity removed, is creating a packing-constrained supply picture rather than a production-constrained one. That distinction matters this week: when packing capacity comes back online, the relief valve opens fast. Feeder cattle, meanwhile, closed Friday at $366.60, barely changed, off just 0.2%. The feeder/live spread is still telling you feedlots have pricing power on the sell side but are cautious on the buy side. The U.S.-Iran MOU (Memorandum of Understanding pausing the conflict, announced late last week per Frayne Olson of NDSU Extension) is starting to ease input cost pressures in the broader economy, which is constructive for feed margins over time. Watch for any resolution signal at the Fort Morgan plant; that is the event that resets the live cattle chart faster than any USDA report this week.
Packing constraint, not herd shortage, is driving the high. Resolution ends it.
⛽ENERGY FIRMS, METALS FALLMEDIUM CONVICTION
WTI crude closed Friday at $76.54, up 2.7% on the session. The Iran-Hormuz tensions, with the Strait of Hormuz premium that built since early April now deflating on diplomatic progress, produced a counterintuitive day: crude firmed as the MOU news crystallized and Iraq's announcement that it will maintain its Syria-Baniyas export route regardless of Hormuz status added a structural supply story to the mix. Diesel and input costs for the 2026 crop are more tied to what crude does over the next 60 days than to any single session. Natural gas added 1.3% to $3.20, modest, with Permian gas production running well above prior-year pace. Gold fell hard at $4,173, down 3.8%, and silver dropped 6.3% to $64.91. Both metals have been breaking since mid-week; when the dollar firms and diplomatic progress reduces the safe-haven bid, precious metals give back fast. The Dollar Index closed at $100.85, up 0.6%.
Crude up on supply route news, not demand; metals falling with safe-haven unwind.

🧠 THE MORE YOU KNOW
Pollination week and corn: why the next 10 days matter more than any WASDE
Corn at $4.17 is sitting 43% off its 52-week low with no weather premium priced in. That reflects a specific bet: that the cool, wet pattern forecast for the Heartland carries through the pollination window, roughly the last week of June into the first week of July for the core Belt. Corn pollination is the one irreversible event in the crop calendar. A plant that runs out of pollen receptivity during a heat or drought stress cannot recover yield the way it can recover from early-season flooding. A single week of 95-degree temperatures with no rainfall during silk emergence can take 20 to 30 bushels per acre off a field that looked fine in June. The reason no weather premium exists today is that the current forecast does not show that scenario. But the 11-to-15-day models are where that can change. Watch the extended forecasts starting Wednesday. If the pattern flips to hot and dry in the July 1 to 5 window, corn at $4.17 will not stay there.
