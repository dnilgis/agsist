ACREAGE LANDED; CORN FINDS $4.14 AND WAITS

Stocks surprise beat softened the acreage blow, but December corn at $4.38 is still pricing acres, not heat.

🧵 WED UPDATEDoes Tuesday's USDA Acreage report confirm planted corn and soybean acres at levels that hold current futures prices, or does a surprise reprice the board?
⚡Overnight Surprise: Oats DN 3.1%

The USDA Acreage report landed yesterday, and today corn is telling you exactly what it thinks: $4.14 nearby, $4.38 December, and almost no movement in either direction. The quarterly stocks number came in 14% above last year, which should have been bearish on its own, but Compeer Financial's Megan Roberts flagged what actually happened: stocks came in below trade expectations, and the below-expectations read flipped the sentiment on its head. So corn is not in freefall. It is also not rallying. It is sitting on a data verdict while 90-to-95 degree heat blankets the Corn Belt during the most critical week of pollination, and the December contract is not pricing any of that yet.

A 14% year-over-year jump in corn stocks would normally hit futures hard. It didn't, because the trade was positioned for an even larger number and the stocks figure came in below expectations. That spread between bearish math and bullish surprise is the reason corn is at $4.14 and not $3.95 today. The lesson: in grain markets, the absolute number matters less than where it landed versus what the funds were betting.

🌽Corn: Heat Meets the Data VerdictMEDIUM CONVICTION
📡DRIVERUSDA quarterly stocks below trade expectations offset bearish acreage; Corn Belt heat wave enters pollination window.
↺Corn: held well above $3.95 target; stocks surprise changed the post-acreage math.
Corn nearby settled at $4.14, off less than a quarter-cent. December lost 1.5 cents to $4.38. Yesterday's Acreage report was bearish on acres, but the quarterly stocks read saved the session: stocks came in 14% above a year ago in absolute terms, yet below where the trade was positioned, and that below-expectations print flipped the tone. Now the market has its data verdict and is staring directly at a Corn Belt heat wave, with highs of 90 to 95 degrees forecast through the week in most areas east of the southern High Plains. That is peak pollination weather. Heat stress during pollination is the one thing that can reprice the December contract without a single USDA report, and $4.38 is not pricing any stress premium right now. The 6-to-10 day outlook keeps hotter-than-normal conditions in place nationally. If the heat holds and the upper Midwest rain coverage thins, December has room to move toward $4.50 without a single new bushel of news.
Data verdict absorbed; next move belongs to the thermometer, not the calendar.
🫘Soybeans: November Slips, Nearby HoldsLOW CONVICTION
📡DRIVERSoyoil/meal complex continues post-run pullback; China purchase commitment not yet showing in new-crop bids.
Nearby soybeans barely moved, settling at $11.14, up half a cent. November gave back 5.5 cents to $11.44. The split matters: old-crop is essentially unchanged while new-crop is softening, and that is carry behavior, not a rally. Soybean meal dropped 0.7% to $303.20 and soybean oil fell 1.1% to $64.83, continuing the pullback from last week's soyoil run. The same Corn Belt heat pattern that threatens corn pollination also accelerates soybean pod-fill timing, but soybeans are less vulnerable at this calendar point than corn. China's $17 billion annual US ag purchase commitment through 2028, announced May 18, has yet to translate into a sustained futures bid past the initial bounce, and the November contract at $11.44 is drifting rather than building. No clean catalyst for today's new-crop softness. Watch Thursday's export sales: below 300,000 metric tons keeps November under pressure.
Old-crop steady, new-crop drifting; Thursday export sales are the next real read.
🎯 Producers with unpriced November beans: $11.44 is carry, not a signal. Hold and watch Thursday's export number before deciding.

📡DRIVERCargill Fort Morgan/Schuyler lockout ongoing, no resolution; processing-constrained dynamics continue to cap live and feeder recovery.
↺Cattle: same processing-drag pattern, no new resolution signal.
Live cattle settled at $242.25, off 0.5%. Feeders gave back 0.8% to $364.35. The ongoing Cargill Fort Morgan/Schuyler plant lockout that began Tuesday May 19 is still the load-bearing story here: 1,700 workers out, roughly 6,000 head per day removed from processing, and no resolution announced. The box beef cutout has not produced a fresh catalyst to break the pattern either way. Live cattle have now been grinding in the low $240s since the lockout created processing-constrained dynamics three weeks ago, and the chart damage done in May has not been repaired. Feeders at $364.35 remain near their session range ceiling but have not reclaimed the levels seen before the lockout began. The feeder-to-live ratio at current prices sits near 1.50, historically wide, and that spread keeps telling you the market is not done repricing the processing constraint. No resolution signal from Cargill means wait is the right verb.
Lockout still sets the ceiling; live cattle cannot rally sustainably until processing capacity comes back.

🧠 THE MORE YOU KNOW
The Pollination Heat Premium Has a Short Shelf Life
December corn at $4.38 is sitting inside peak pollination week with 90-to-95 degree heat across most of the Belt and no stress premium in the price. That gap exists because markets price probabilities, not outcomes: the heat has to actually show up in crop condition ratings before funds buy the weather story. The window where a heat premium can be added to December corn is roughly two weeks, from now through mid-July, after which the critical pollination period closes and the weather risk window shuts. After that, the only levers left are the August WASDE production estimate and export pace. Producers watching December corn at $4.38 should understand they are watching a clock, not just a price: if this week's heat does not show up in Monday's Crop Progress ratings as a condition deterioration, the premium window closes without ever opening.

The U.S. commercial poultry sector has been managing its own H5N1 exposure since 2022; any spread in Australia's commercial layer flock adds to global egg supply pressure and keeps domestic biosecurity protocols in focus.

For row-crop rotation managers in the Mid-South, the cotton-over-rice pivot is already reshaping what gets planted next spring.
