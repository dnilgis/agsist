ACREAGE DAY, HORMUZ FLARES, SOYOIL RUNS 6.9%

The USDA Acreage report lands at 11 AM CT Tuesday; overnight, fresh U.S.-Iran strikes rattled Hormuz tanker traffic and soybean oil had its biggest session in weeks.

📊 THE NUMBER
60%
share of U.S. soybeans now crushed domestically
Three or four years ago, over 60% of U.S. soybeans were exported. The National Oilseed Processors Association says that ratio has now flipped, with domestic crush absorbing the majority. That structural shift is exactly why a Hormuz-driven soyoil rally hits the board this hard: the domestic biofuel demand chain is now the primary customer, not a Chinese cargo ship. When Hormuz rattles palm oil supplies, domestic soyoil fills the gap and the futures board reprices it immediately.

🌽CORN BREAKS HARD BEFORE ACREAGEHIGH CONVICTION
📡DRIVERPre-Acreage fund liquidation in old-crop; USDA Acreage report due 11 AM CT Tuesday.
Corn nearby dropped 10.5 cents to $4.13 and that move is almost entirely pre-Acreage positioning. December held at $4.42, off just a penny, and that split is the tell: the funds are lightening old-crop exposure ahead of Tuesday's 11 AM print, not making a directional call on the new crop. The Acreage report could show fewer planted acres than the March estimate, which would add premium to December, or it could confirm the big number that the wet-May planting pace suggested. Iowa and Missouri farmers caught dry windows between rain systems and pushed corn planting hard through late May, so a number above 94 million acres is very much in play. If Tuesday comes in above 94 million, the December contract at $4.42 is the first line the market tests lower. If it comes in below 93 million, watch for the new-crop to run back toward $4.55 in a hurry. A North Iowa grain farmer told Brownfield last week there are 'few if any pathways to profitability this year.' At $4.13 nearby, he is not wrong.
Old-crop fear is priced; new-crop waits on Tuesday's acre count.
🎯 If you have unpriced old-crop corn in storage and local basis is firm, price today. Do not wait through the Acreage print with unhedged bushels.
🫘SOYOIL RUNS; HORMUZ FLARES AGAINHIGH CONVICTION
📡DRIVERWeekend U.S.-Iran strikes; Strait of Hormuz tanker traffic slowing per oilprice.com; domestic soyoil demand base from RFS finalized RVOs.
Soybean oil had its biggest single session in weeks, running 6.9% to $71.30 after fresh U.S.-Iran strikes this weekend slowed tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz, the Iran-Hormuz tensions that built since early April and briefly appeared to be deflating on diplomatic progress have reignited sharply. Pakistan's state LNG importer was out seeking emergency cargo as of Monday morning and vessel owners are pulling back from Hormuz transits. That tightens global vegetable oil flows and domestic soyoil fills the gap fast. Soybean meal also firmed, up 1.3% to $307.00, so the crush is running both sides of the board higher. Soybeans nearby gave back 23 cents to $11.26 on old-crop selling, but November beans actually added 6.75 cents to $11.56: the market is telling you the new-crop demand picture looks better than the old-crop carry. The domestic biofuel shift matters here. With roughly 60% of U.S. soybeans now going to domestic crush rather than export, a Hormuz disruption that tightens palm oil supply hits the soyoil board directly and the RFS demand base amplifies it.
Soyoil is the Hormuz trade right now; November beans are acting better than nearby.
🎯 Producers holding unpriced new-crop beans: the November contract at $11.56 is the cleanest level of the week. Consider layering in sales on any Tuesday pop from a friendly Acreage number before the heat dome story fully prices.

🐄CATTLE STEADY; HOGS FALL HARDMEDIUM CONVICTION
📡DRIVERCargill Fort Morgan lockout ongoing, processing-constrained cattle dynamics; hog fund liquidation with no fresh demand catalyst.
Live cattle eased to $245.82, off 0.6%, and feeders gave back 0.9% to $369.85. The ongoing Cargill Fort Morgan/Schuyler plant lockout that began May 19 has kept the fed cattle trade in a processing-constrained posture for six weeks now, and the market is not finding new sellers at these levels even with box beef softer. The cattle complex is not acting healthy, but it is not breaking either. Hogs are a different story. Lean hogs dropped 3.9% to $92.92, their sharpest single-session move in recent weeks. Hog markets have been drifting and today's break is the kind of move that happens when the funds run out of reasons to stay long in a seasonally weak period without a fresh demand catalyst. Class III Milk also fell 2.5% to $16.01, consistent with its multi-week drift lower. No single news item drove hogs today; this looks like fund liquidation in a market that has been losing conviction for two weeks.
Cattle holding but not healthy; hogs broke hard on fund exit, not a news event.

WTI crude fell 1.9% to $69.23 even as the Strait of Hormuz situation re-escalated this weekend. That divergence is worth reading carefully: crude lost roughly 19% during May as the Hormuz premium unwound on diplomatic progress, and Monday's fresh U.S.-Iran strikes brought the premium back, but the crude market's reaction was muted while soyoil ran hard. Middle East oil production rebounding to between 14.6 and 15 million barrels per day is the offset: more supply is coming to market even as the transit risk returns. Natural gas eased 1.6% to $3.23. The LNG side of the story is louder: Pakistan sought emergency cargo Monday morning, and China's June LNG imports are running above May levels on summer power demand. The $5.5 billion Williams/Momentum Midstream deal in late-stage talks signals the midstream sector still sees long-term domestic gas infrastructure as a growth trade regardless of near-term price softness.
Crude muted despite Hormuz flare; the soyoil market read the geopolitics faster than crude did.

🧠 THE MORE YOU KNOW
The heat dome arriving this week is a pollination clock, not just a weather headline.
With corn nearby at $4.13 and no weather premium in the December contract at $4.42, the market is not yet pricing the heat dome building over the Belt. Here is why that could change fast: corn is a narrow-window crop at pollination. A sustained stretch above 95 degrees Fahrenheit during silking, roughly a 10-to-14-day window that varies by planting date and geography, can cut yield by 3% to 8% per day of heat stress. The 2026 planting season ran behind the average through May before catching up between rain systems, which means the Belt's pollination window is slightly later than average this year. That delay puts peak pollination exposure squarely in early-to-mid July, right when this heat dome is forecast to arrive. If the heat dome stalls rather than moves through, the December contract at $4.42 will not stay quiet for long. Scouts and agronomists should be checking silk emergence dates this week. The Acreage number may reprice supply; the weather map may reprice yield. Both could move the board in the same week.

DISEASE
New Screwworm Cases Threatening Beef Production, Pricing
Congressional Democrats sent a letter to USDA Secretary Rollins demanding transparency and science-driven action on the New World Screwworm as cases push closer to U.S. cattle territory. The livestock disease is a long-tail supply risk that the cattle complex has not begun to price, and the processing-constrained dynamics from the Cargill lockout mean the industry has little buffer if screwworm containment fails.
POLICY
New York and Connecticut Launch State-Level Tariff Relief for Farmers
New York and Connecticut became the first states to open applications for direct tariff relief to farmers, with New York offering up to $25,000 per producer from a $30 million state fund. This is a state-level patch on a federal problem: the North Dakota State study estimated active China tariffs cost U.S. ag $15 billion annually, and no state fund closes that gap, but it is the first visible political response below the federal level.
WEATHER
K-State Expands Corn Leafhopper Monitoring After Oklahoma Detections
Kansas Corn and Kansas State University are deploying expanded monitoring and free diagnostic testing statewide after corn leafhopper detections in Oklahoma this season. Leafhopper pressure during late vegetative and early reproductive stages can accelerate corn streak mosaic virus spread. With pollination approaching across the Belt, this is worth watching if the pressure moves north.
