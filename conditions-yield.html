Questions people actually ask

Do USDA crop condition ratings actually predict final yield?
Partially, late, and it depends where you farm. In mid-July (week 28), the Good+Excellent share explains only about 23% of Iowa's final corn yield variation — but by late September it explains roughly 64%. In North Carolina, July ratings already explain 79%. This page computes the number for every state and week instead of asserting it.
Why do the ratings explain so little in early summer?
Because the yield isn't made yet. July corn in Iowa can rate 80% Good+Excellent and then hit August heat during grain fill. Ratings measure how the crop looks; yield is decided by what happens next. The R-squared curve rising through the season is literally the crop's fate being sealed week by week.
What does the slope number mean?
It's the historical relationship: how many bushels per acre of yield deviation (from that state's long-term trend) has come with each 1-point move in Good+Excellent share, at that week. Illinois at week 39: about +0.86 bu/ac per G+E point.
Why detrend the yields?
State yields rise about two bushels per acre per year from genetics and agronomy alone. Without removing that trend, the fit confuses 'years got later' with 'ratings got better' and reports fake signal. We fit ratings against each year's deviation from trend; the raw fit is kept in the data for transparency.
