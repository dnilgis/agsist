üåΩ UNDERSTANDING CORN BASIS

What is Basis? Basis is the difference between your local cash price and the Chicago futures price:

Cash Price ‚àí Futures Price = Basis

If December corn futures are trading at $4.50 and your local elevator is bidding $4.25, your basis is -25 cents (often written as "25 under").

Why Basis Matters

Basis can add or subtract 20-40 cents per bushel from your final price. On a 500-acre corn farm averaging 190 bu/acre, a 20-cent basis improvement is worth $19,000.

What Affects Basis?

  • Location: Distance to rivers, ethanol plants, and export terminals
  • Time of year: Basis typically weakens at harvest, strengthens in summer
  • Local supply/demand: Nearby ethanol plant demand, local yields
  • Transportation costs: Fuel prices, rail availability, truck freight
  • Storage availability: On-farm vs commercial storage capacity

üìà WHEN TO SELL YOUR GRAIN

The "best" time to sell is when the price exceeds your break-even by a profitable margin. But understanding seasonal patterns helps you set realistic expectations.

Seasonal Price Patterns (Historical)

  • Late June - Early July: Often the highest prices due to weather premium before pollination
  • August: Prices often decline as crop conditions become clearer
  • October - November: Typically the lowest prices (harvest pressure)
  • January - March: Modest recovery as farmer selling slows
  • April - May: Planting concerns can add weather premium

Scale-Up Selling Strategy

Instead of trying to pick the perfect top, many successful marketers use a scale-up approach:

At break-even + 10%Sell 20%
At break-even + 20%Sell 25%
At break-even + 30%Sell 25%
RemainingSell by next harvest

Market-Moving Events to Watch

  • WASDE Report (monthly): Supply/demand estimates
  • Prospective Plantings (late March): Acreage intentions
  • Acreage Report (late June): Actual planted acres
  • Grain Stocks (quarterly): Inventory levels

üõ°Ô∏è YP VS RP CROP INSURANCE

Choosing between Yield Protection (YP) and Revenue Protection (RP) is one of the most important crop insurance decisions you'll make each year.

Quick Comparison

Feature Yield Protection (YP) Revenue Protection (RP)
Covers Yield loss only Yield + Price loss
Price used Harvest price Higher of spring or harvest
Premium cost Lower (10-15% less) Higher
Best when... Prices expected stable/rising Price drops are a concern

When RP Makes Sense

  • Spring prices are historically high (more downside risk)
  • You're highly leveraged and can't afford price risk
  • You haven't pre-sold a significant portion of your crop
  • Trade policy or global supply concerns exist

When YP Might Work

  • Spring prices are historically low (limited downside)
  • You've already locked in prices through forward contracts
  • Premium savings are significant for your operation
  • You have strong financial reserves

Key Deadlines

Check our USDA Calendar for current year deadlines:

  • Sales Closing: March 15 (most spring crops)
  • Acreage Reporting: July 15
  • Production Reporting: Varies by crop

Growing Degree Units (GDUs), also called heat units, measure the heat accumulation that drives corn development from planting to maturity.

The Formula

GDU = [(Daily High + Daily Low) √∑ 2] ‚àí 50

Important rules:

  • Cap the high temperature at 86¬∞F (corn growth slows above this)
  • Floor the low temperature at 50¬∞F (no growth below this)
  • If the result is negative, GDU = 0 for that day

üí∞ BREAK-EVEN ANALYSIS FOR FARMERS

Your break-even price is the most important number in your grain marketing plan. If you don't know your cost per bushel, you're flying blind.

The Formula

Break-Even Price = Total Cost per Acre √∑ Expected Yield

Costs to Include

DIRECT COSTS

  • Seed
  • Fertilizer (N, P, K, micronutrients)
  • Chemicals (herbicide, insecticide, fungicide)
  • Crop insurance premium
  • Drying and handling
  • Custom hire / application

OVERHEAD COSTS

  • Land rent or ownership cost
  • Machinery depreciation
  • Fuel and repairs
  • Labor (including unpaid family labor!)
  • Interest on operating loans
  • General overhead (insurance, utilities, etc.)

Using Break-Even for Marketing

  • Above break-even + 20%: Strong sell signal ‚Äî lock in profits
  • Above break-even: Consider selling 25-50% to cover costs
  • At break-even: Cover operating expenses at minimum
  • Below break-even: Evaluate storage, basis improvement, insurance options
