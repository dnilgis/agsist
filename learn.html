üìä Understanding Corn Basis

Basis is the difference between your local cash price and the futures price. It's the single most important concept in grain marketing that most farmers don't fully understand.

The Formula:
Basis = Local Cash Price ‚àí Futures Price

Why Basis Matters

When you sell grain, you're not really selling at "the futures price." You're selling at futures plus or minus basis. A strong basis can add 20-30 cents to your price. A weak basis can cost you the same.

What Affects Basis?

  • Transportation costs ‚Äì Further from export terminals = weaker basis
  • Local supply/demand ‚Äì Ethanol plant nearby? Stronger basis
  • Storage availability ‚Äì Full elevators = weaker basis
  • Time of year ‚Äì Harvest = weakest basis, summer = strongest
  • Quality ‚Äì High test weight, low moisture = basis premiums
Example
December corn futures: $4.50/bu
Local elevator bid: $4.25/bu
Basis = $4.25 ‚àí $4.50 = ‚àí25 cents (25 under)

Basis Patterns

Basis is seasonal and somewhat predictable. Track your local basis over several years to find patterns. Typically:

  • Weakest: October-November (harvest pressure)
  • Improving: December-February (storage carries value)
  • Strongest: May-August (old crop tight, new crop uncertain)

Trading Basis

You can lock in futures price with a hedge, then wait for basis to improve before setting your final cash price. This is called "basis trading" and it separates professional grain marketers from everyone else.

üí∞ When to Sell Your Grain

There's no perfect answer, but there are smart strategies. The goal isn't to hit the high‚Äîit's to average a profitable price across your crop.

The 3 Worst Times to Sell

  1. Harvest ‚Äì Everyone's selling. Basis is weakest. You have the least information about final yield.
  2. When you "need" cash ‚Äì Desperation selling never works out.
  3. After a big rally ‚Äì By the time it's on the news, the move is mostly done.

A Simple Marketing Plan

Sell in increments. Don't try to time the market‚Äîspread your risk:

  • Sell 10-20% pre-harvest when prices hit your target
  • Sell 20-30% at harvest for cash flow
  • Store 30-40% for basis improvement
  • Keep 10-20% for spring rallies
Rule of Thumb:
If the price covers your costs + reasonable profit, sell some. Don't wait for "more."

Watch These Signals

  • USDA Reports ‚Äì WASDE, Grain Stocks, Prospective Plantings move markets
  • South American weather ‚Äì Brazil/Argentina crops affect US prices
  • Export sales ‚Äì Strong weekly sales = bullish
  • Basis trends ‚Äì Strengthening basis = good time to price
  • Your break-even ‚Äì Know your number before you sell

Seasonal Patterns

Corn and soybeans have historical price patterns:

  • Corn lows: Often October (harvest) and June (big acreage fears)
  • Corn highs: Often July (weather market) or December-January
  • Soy lows: October harvest, sometimes February
  • Soy highs: Often May-July (SA crop + US weather)

But patterns break. Always have a plan, not just a hope.

üõ°Ô∏è Crop Insurance 101

Crop insurance is the safety net that lets you take risks. Understanding your options can save‚Äîor cost‚Äîyou thousands.

The Two Main Types

  • Yield Protection (YP) ‚Äì Covers yield loss only. You're exposed to price drops.
  • Revenue Protection (RP) ‚Äì Covers revenue (yield √ó price). Protects against yield loss AND price drops. Most popular choice.
RP with Harvest Price Option:
If harvest price > spring price, your guarantee goes UP. This is why 90% of corn/soy acres are on RP.

Coverage Levels

You choose 50% to 85% coverage. Higher coverage = higher premium, but better protection.

  • 85% ‚Äì Maximum protection. Makes sense for cash rent situations.
  • 80% ‚Äì Good balance of cost vs. coverage.
  • 75% or below ‚Äì Only for very low-risk situations or tight budgets.

Key Dates (Upper Midwest)

  • March 15 ‚Äì Sales closing date. Last day to buy or change coverage.
  • July 15 ‚Äì Acreage reporting deadline.
  • Premium due ‚Äì Usually October, after harvest.

The Enterprise Unit Discount

Combining all your acres of a crop into one "enterprise unit" gives you a 20-30% premium discount. The trade-off: you can't file on individual fields‚Äîyour whole-farm average must drop below guarantee.

Prevented Planting

If you can't plant due to weather, prevented planting coverage pays 55% (YP) or 60% (RP) of your guarantee. Some years this matters a lot.

üå°Ô∏è Growing Degree Units (GDUs) Explained

GDUs measure heat accumulation that drives corn development. Understanding GDUs helps you pick hybrids, predict maturity, and make harvest decisions.

The Formula

Daily GDU = (High + Low) √∑ 2 ‚àí 50

Rules:
‚Ä¢ High capped at 86¬∞F (corn growth slows above this)
‚Ä¢ Low floored at 50¬∞F (no growth below this)
‚Ä¢ If result is negative, GDU = 0 for that day
Example
High: 82¬∞F, Low: 58¬∞F
GDU = (82 + 58) √∑ 2 ‚àí 50 = 70 ‚àí 50 = 20 GDUs

GDU Requirements by Hybrid

  • 85-day hybrid: ~2,100 GDUs to black layer
  • 95-day hybrid: ~2,400 GDUs
  • 105-day hybrid: ~2,700 GDUs
  • 115-day hybrid: ~3,000 GDUs

GDUs for Growth Stages

  • Emergence: 115-120 GDUs after planting
  • Silking (R1): ~1,200-1,400 GDUs (about 50% of total)
  • Black layer (R6): Full maturity, kernel moisture ~30-35%

Why This Matters

  • Hybrid selection: Match GDU requirement to your area's typical accumulation
  • Planting date: Late planting = fewer GDUs available = need shorter hybrid
  • Fall frost risk: Track GDUs to predict if crop will mature before frost
  • Silage timing: Use GDUs to predict optimal harvest moisture

Average Daily GDUs (May-September)

  • Southern Wisconsin: 18-20 GDUs/day
  • Central Wisconsin: 16-18 GDUs/day
  • Northern Wisconsin: 14-16 GDUs/day
  • Southern Minnesota: 19-21 GDUs/day
  • Central Iowa: 22-24 GDUs/day

üìà Break-Even Analysis for Farmers

Your break-even price is the minimum you need to cover costs. Know this number before you sell a single bushel.

The Formula

Break-Even Price = Total Cost per Acre √∑ Expected Yield
Example - Corn
Total costs: $850/acre
Expected yield: 190 bu/acre
Break-even = $850 √∑ 190 = $4.47/bu

Costs to Include

Direct Costs (per acre):

  • Seed: $100-150
  • Fertilizer: $150-200
  • Chemical: $40-60
  • Crop insurance: $30-50
  • Drying: $30-50

Overhead Costs (per acre):

  • Land (rent or ownership): $150-300
  • Machinery: $80-120
  • Labor: $30-50
  • Interest & overhead: $30-50

Know Your Numbers

Most farmers underestimate costs. Common mistakes:

  • Forgetting unpaid family labor
  • Ignoring machinery depreciation
  • Not counting interest on operating loans
  • Using "average" yield when you should use APH

Using Break-Even for Marketing

  • Above break-even + 20%: Strong sell signal. Lock in profits.
  • Above break-even: Consider selling 25-50%.
  • At break-even: Cover operating costs at minimum.
  • Below break-even: Evaluate options carefully. Storage? Insurance?

Break-Even by Scenario

Smart marketers calculate multiple break-evens:

  • Cash flow break-even: Just covers out-of-pocket costs
  • Full break-even: Covers all costs including depreciation
  • Profit target: Break-even + your desired profit margin
