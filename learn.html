CH 1 Mastering Basis ‚Äî The Local Price Component

Basis is one of the most important ‚Äî and most misunderstood ‚Äî concepts in grain marketing. If you only learn one thing from this guide, understanding basis will have the biggest impact on your profitability. According to Purdue University's Center for Commercial Agriculture, farmers who actively track and manage basis can improve returns by 15-30 cents per bushel over those who ignore it.

What Is Basis?

Basis is simply the difference between your local cash price and the futures price:

Cash Price ‚àí Futures Price = Basis
A negative basis means local prices are below futures; a positive basis means local prices exceed futures
üìù Example: Calculating Your Basis

December corn futures are trading at $4.50/bu. Your local elevator is bidding $4.25/bu for December delivery.

$4.25 (cash) ‚àí $4.50 (futures) = ‚àí$0.25 basis
This is often written as "25 under" or "-25¬¢"

If the same elevator is bidding $4.60 when futures are at $4.50, your basis would be +10¬¢ or "10 over."

Many farmers focus exclusively on futures prices, but basis can easily swing your final price by 20-50 cents per bushel. Consider the math:

That 20-cent difference ‚Äî which is common between harvest lows and summer highs ‚Äî is worth $19,000 on a typical Upper Midwest corn operation. Understanding when basis is historically strong versus weak at YOUR elevator is critical.

What Affects Basis? The 5 Key Factors

Basis reflects local supply and demand conditions that differ from the national/global picture captured in futures prices. University of Wisconsin Extension identifies five primary factors:

  1. Location & Transportation Costs: Distance to terminal markets, ethanol plants, and export facilities matters enormously. Elevators near the Mississippi River, major rail lines, or ethanol plants typically offer stronger basis because they have lower transportation costs to move grain. An elevator 100 miles from a river terminal must discount its bid to cover trucking costs.
  2. Local Supply & Demand: A new ethanol plant opening nearby can strengthen basis by 10-20 cents as it competes for bushels. Conversely, a bumper crop in your county while others had drought can weaken local basis even as futures rise. This is why YOUR basis pattern may differ significantly from state or regional averages.
  3. Time of Year (Seasonality): Basis typically weakens during harvest when supplies are abundant and storage is tight, then strengthens through winter and into summer as supplies tighten. This seasonal pattern is generally predictable and tradeable.
  4. Storage Availability: When commercial storage fills up at harvest, elevators widen basis (offer worse prices) to discourage deliveries. Farmers with on-farm storage can capture this basis improvement by storing grain and selling later when basis strengthens.
  5. Quality Factors: Basis reflects quality premiums and discounts. High-moisture corn, low test weight, or damage will result in weaker basis (larger discounts). Conversely, premium quality may earn a basis premium at certain buyers.

üí° Key Insight: Basis Can Be More Predictable Than Futures

Futures prices are influenced by global factors ‚Äî trade wars, weather in Brazil, currency movements ‚Äî that are nearly impossible to predict. But YOUR local basis follows more predictable seasonal patterns driven by local supply/demand. This means basis-based strategies (like storage hedging) can be more reliable than trying to guess futures direction.

How to Track & Use Basis

Step 1: Build Your Basis History

Record your local elevator's cash bids weekly, along with the relevant futures price, for at least 3-5 years. This creates YOUR specific basis history, which is far more valuable than regional averages. Free tools like Purdue's Crop Basis Tool can supplement your records with regional data.

Step 2: Identify Your "Normal" Basis Range

Calculate your average basis for each month or season. Then identify the historical range (best and worst). This tells you when current basis is historically strong (good time to lock in basis) or weak (wait if possible).

Step 3: Make Basis-Informed Decisions

Iowa State Extension's Ag Decision Maker recommends using basis outlook alongside futures outlook to choose marketing tools:

Basis Bearish ‚Üë Rise ‚Üì Weaken Basis contract ‚Äî lock in basis, leave futures open
Most Bullish ‚Üë Rise ‚Üë Strengthen Store unpriced; wait for higher prices

Advanced: Basis Contracts Explained

A basis contract allows you to lock in the basis component while leaving the futures price to be set at a time you choose before delivery. The elevator typically pays a portion of the value at delivery, with final settlement when you price.

üìù Example: Basis Contract in Action

In June, your elevator offers a basis contract for fall delivery at ‚àí15¬¢ basis to December futures. Historically, your harvest basis averages ‚àí35¬¢, so this is 20 cents better than normal.

You sign a basis contract for 5,000 bushels. You'll deliver in October, and your final price will be:

December Futures Price (at time you choose to price) ‚àí 15¬¢ = Your Cash Price

If December futures are $4.50 when you set your price, you receive $4.35/bu. If futures rally to $5.00, you receive $4.85/bu. Either way, you've locked in that favorable 15-under basis.

CH 2 Futures Markets 101 ‚Äî How They Work

Futures markets serve two essential functions: price discovery (establishing a transparent, global price for commodities) and risk transfer (allowing producers and buyers to manage price risk). For farmers, futures provide a way to lock in prices before harvest ‚Äî or to understand where the market expects prices to be.

What Is a Futures Contract?

A futures contract is a standardized, legally binding agreement to buy or sell a specific quantity and quality of a commodity at a predetermined price on a future date. Key characteristics:

  • Standardized: Every corn futures contract represents exactly 5,000 bushels of #2 Yellow corn
  • Exchange-traded: Contracts trade on the CME Group (formerly Chicago Board of Trade), providing transparency and liquidity
  • Marked-to-market: Gains and losses are settled daily, requiring margin deposits
  • Delivery or offset: Contracts can be fulfilled by physical delivery OR closed out by taking an opposite position (most are offset, not delivered)

These are the official specifications from CME Group for the most commonly traded grain futures:

üìä Understanding Tick Values

A "tick" is the minimum price movement. For corn, the minimum move is ¬º cent per bushel. On a 5,000-bushel contract: $0.0025 √ó 5,000 = $12.50 per tick. So if corn moves from $4.50 to $4.51 (4 ticks), your contract gains or loses $50.

Contract Months: Which to Use?

Futures contracts trade for specific delivery months. You should generally trade the contract month closest to (but after) when you plan to sell your physical grain:

Crop Contract Months Most Relevant for Upper Midwest
Corn Mar, May, Jul, Sep, Dec December for new-crop; July for old-crop
Soybeans Jan, Mar, May, Jul, Aug, Sep, Nov November for new-crop; July for old-crop
Wheat Mar, May, Jul, Sep, Dec July for winter wheat harvest

Why December corn? December is the first contract month after typical Midwest harvest (October-November). It's the "new-crop" contract that most closely aligns with harvest-time pricing.

How Hedging Works: A Short Hedge Example

Farmers use a "short hedge" (selling futures) to protect against falling prices. Here's the concept:

üìù Example: Hedging 10,000 Bushels of Corn

In May: You expect to harvest 10,000 bushels in October. December corn futures are at $4.80. You're happy with this price but worried it might fall.

Action: SELL 2 December corn futures contracts (2 √ó 5,000 = 10,000 bushels) at $4.80.

In October: Futures have fallen to $4.30. Your local elevator bids $4.05 (‚àí25¬¢ basis).

Close the hedge: BUY 2 December corn futures at $4.30. Profit on futures = $0.50/bu √ó 10,000 = $5,000

Cash sale: 10,000 bu √ó $4.05 = $40,500
Futures gain: $5,000
Net price received: $45,500 √∑ 10,000 = $4.55/bu

Without hedging, you'd have received only $4.05/bu. The hedge added $0.50/bu to your realized price.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Hedging Doesn't Guarantee a Specific Price

Notice the farmer didn't receive exactly $4.80 (the original futures price). The final price depends on basis at delivery. Hedging locks in the futures component of price while leaving you exposed to basis risk. This is why understanding basis (Chapter 1) is so critical.

Margin Requirements

Futures trading requires posting "margin" ‚Äî a good-faith deposit that ensures you can cover potential losses. There are two types:

  • Initial Margin: The deposit required to open a position (~$1,500-2,500 per corn contract)
  • Maintenance Margin: The minimum balance you must maintain (~$1,200-2,000 per contract)

If losses push your account below maintenance margin, you'll receive a "margin call" requiring immediate deposit of additional funds. This is why many farmers prefer grain contracts through their elevator (forward, basis, HTA) rather than direct futures trading ‚Äî the elevator handles the hedging and margin requirements.

CH 3 Grain Contract Types ‚Äî Your Marketing Tools

Most farmers don't trade futures directly. Instead, they use grain contracts offered by local elevators and processors. These contracts embed futures-based pricing mechanisms while handling the complexity (margin, delivery logistics) for you. According to Iowa State Extension, understanding your contract options is essential to building a complete marketing plan.

"You want to have a wide variety of tools ‚Äî sell for cash, sell forward, hedge, basis contract ‚Äî on your marketing menu," advises ISU Extension economist Chad Hart. "Given the volatility we see in market prices, the more choices farmers have, the better off they are."

Deliver grain today, receive today's posted price. Simple and immediate.

üìù Forward Contract

Lock in a complete price (futures + basis) for delivery at a future date.

Lock in basis while leaving futures price to set later. Ideal when basis is historically strong.

üìà Hedge-to-Arrive (HTA)

Lock in futures price while leaving basis to set at delivery.

‚è∞ Delayed Price (DP)

Forward Contract (Cash Forward)

A forward contract locks in a complete price (combining futures and basis) for delivery at a specified future date. This is the most common pre-harvest pricing tool.

üìù Example: Forward Contract

In March, December corn futures are $4.80 and your elevator's harvest basis is ‚àí25¬¢. Transaction fee is 5¬¢.

Forward price = $4.80 ‚àí $0.25 ‚àí $0.05 = $4.50/bu

You sign a forward contract for 10,000 bushels at $4.50 for October delivery. Regardless of where prices go, you'll receive $4.50/bu when you deliver.

  • When to use: When both futures AND basis are at acceptable levels; when you want complete price certainty
  • Advantages: Complete price protection; simple to understand; no margin calls
  • Disadvantages: Gives up ALL upside if prices rally; production risk if you can't deliver
‚ö†Ô∏è Production Risk Warning

Forward contracts obligate delivery. If you forward contract 50,000 bushels but only harvest 40,000 (drought, hail, etc.), you must buy 10,000 bushels at market price to fulfill the contract. Never forward contract more than 50-70% of conservative yield estimates.

Basis Contract

A basis contract locks in the basis component while leaving the futures price to be set at a time you choose before delivery.

Final Price = Futures Price (when you set it) + Locked Basis
Example: Basis locked at ‚àí20¬¢, you price when futures are $4.60, you receive $4.40/bu
  • When to use: When basis is historically strong but you're uncertain about futures direction, or believe futures may rise
  • Advantages: Captures favorable basis; maintains futures upside; protects against basis weakening
  • Disadvantages: Exposed to futures price decline; must eventually set price; delivery required

Hedge-to-Arrive (HTA)

An HTA contract locks in the futures price while leaving the basis to be set at delivery. It's the opposite of a basis contract.

Final Price = Locked Futures Price ‚àí Basis (at delivery)
Example: Locked futures at $4.80, delivery basis is ‚àí30¬¢, you receive $4.50/bu
  • When to use: When futures prices are historically high, but basis is currently weak and expected to improve (typical at harvest)
  • Advantages: Locks in favorable futures price; can benefit from basis improvement
  • Disadvantages: Exposed to basis risk; delivery required; may have rolling fees

Decision Framework: Which Contract to Use?

Your Situation Recommended Contract(s)
Happy with current price, want certainty Forward contract or cash sale
Basis historically strong, futures uncertain Basis contract
Futures favorable, basis weak (harvest) HTA contract
Need to move grain, don't want to price Delayed price (with caution)
Want protection but keep upside Minimum price or put options
‚úÖ Best Practice: Diversify Your Contracts

Don't put all your bushels in one basket. A sound marketing plan uses multiple contract types across multiple time periods. For example: 20% forward contracted pre-plant, 20% HTA at summer highs, 30% basis contract, 30% stored for post-harvest opportunities.

CH 4 When to Sell ‚Äî Seasonal Patterns & Timing

Timing matters in grain marketing. According to University of Wisconsin Extension, "nine years out of ten, corn prices bottom during fall harvest" ‚Äî meaning selling at harvest typically leaves money on the table. Understanding seasonal price patterns can help you identify windows when prices are historically strongest and plan your marketing accordingly.

The Seasonal Price Cycle

Grain prices follow predictable seasonal patterns driven by the crop production cycle. CME Group's educational materials explain: "With corn, the most pronounced seasonal trend is the tendency for prices to be near their highest level around July because of the uncertainty around new crop production, then to decline from mid-summer into the harvest season."

Here's the typical annual cycle for corn:

Time Period What's Happening Price Tendency
January - February South American weather focus; export demand Often stable; may rally on S.A. weather
March - April Planting intentions; early fieldwork Often strengthening ‚Äî "buying acres"
May - June Planting progress; early crop conditions Weather premium building
Late June - July Pollination; critical yield determination SEASONAL HIGH ‚Äî weather premium peaks
August - September Crop size becomes clearer; early harvest Declining as uncertainty reduces
October - November Full harvest; maximum supply pressure SEASONAL LOW ‚Äî harvest pressure
December Harvest wrapping up; tax-year decisions Often recovering from harvest lows
üí° Key Insight: The "July 4th Rule"

University of Wisconsin Extension notes: "Nine years in ten it is a good idea to have your stored grain and any pre-harvest marketing completed by the fourth of July." This captures the essence of seasonal marketing ‚Äî the highest prices typically occur before the new crop's yield potential becomes clear.

Soybeans: A Different Pattern

Soybeans follow a similar but distinct pattern. According to CME Group: "Soybeans tend to follow a pattern where prices begin to decline in the July-August time frame, continuing through 'February break,' before reaching their seasonal highs in the summer."

Key differences from corn:

  • South American competition: Brazil's February-April harvest can pressure U.S. prices during winter
  • Less pronounced post-harvest rally: Unlike corn, soybeans rarely show strong carry signals
  • Summer highs still dominant: June-July remains the typical peak period

Scale-Up Selling: A Practical Strategy

Rather than trying to pick the exact top (which is impossible), successful marketers use "scale-up selling" ‚Äî pricing portions of the crop as prices reach target levels:

üìù Example: Scale-Up Marketing Plan

Your break-even is $4.20/bu. You set the following target levels:

$4.40 (+5%)20%$0.20Lock in base profit
$4.60 (+10%)20%$0.40Good return

If prices hit all four targets, your weighted average price is $4.70/bu on 80% of production ‚Äî a $0.50/bu profit. The remaining 20% stays unpriced for additional upside or post-harvest opportunities.

What Breaks the Pattern?

Seasonal patterns work "most of the time" but not always. Major disruptions include:

  • Drought years (2012, 2021): Prices can rally sharply through harvest as crop losses mount
  • Demand shocks: The 2020 COVID pandemic disrupted normal patterns, with prices rallying into fall
  • Trade disruptions: China tariffs (2018-2019) created atypical price action
  • Global supply shocks: Ukraine war (2022) sent prices to multi-year highs

This is why no single strategy works every year ‚Äî and why maintaining flexibility (never selling 100% early) is crucial.

‚úÖ Practical Guidelines

Before planting: Consider pricing 10-20% if prices exceed break-even by 15%+
May-July: Most aggressive pre-harvest pricing window ‚Äî scale up as prices rise
By July 4: Target 50-70% priced if prices have reached acceptable levels
At harvest: Avoid panic selling into harvest lows if you have storage
Post-harvest: Set spring targets; don't hold past May-June without a plan

CH 5 Storage Economics ‚Äî Does It Pay?

Storage is a marketing tool, not a guarantee of higher prices. The decision to store grain should be based on economics ‚Äî comparing expected price gains against the costs of holding grain. According to Purdue University research, "historic returns above costs for corn storage have been in the range of $0.00 to $0.10 per bushel on average" ‚Äî meaning storage works, but margins are thin.

With elevated interest rates, the cost of tying up capital in stored grain has increased dramatically. University of Kentucky research calculates the impact:

Commercial vs. On-Farm Storage Costs

  • First 3 months: 9-16¬¢/bu flat charge
  • Additional months: 2-4¬¢/bu per month
  • 6-month total: 15-28¬¢/bu (plus interest on your capital)

On-farm storage is typically cheaper because you're not paying the elevator's margin ‚Äî but you assume all quality risk and must account for fixed ownership costs (depreciation, insurance) if evaluating a new bin investment.

Reading the "Carry" Signal

The futures market gives you a signal about whether storage will pay ‚Äî the "carry" between contract months:

Carry = Deferred Futures Price ‚àí Nearby Futures Price
Example: July corn at $4.60, December corn at $4.75 = 15¬¢ carry

University of Wisconsin Extension explains what carry tells you:

  • Strong carry (15-25¬¢+): Market is paying for storage; supplies are ample; consider storing
  • Weak carry (5-10¬¢): Market offers little incentive to store; may not cover costs
  • Inverted (negative): Nearby prices higher than deferred; market wants grain NOW ‚Äî sell immediately
‚ö†Ô∏è Carry Doesn't Guarantee Profits

A 20¬¢ carry between December and July doesn't mean you'll make 20¬¢ by storing. You capture carry through basis improvement, but futures prices may fall, offsetting basis gains. The carry signal is about storage incentive, not guaranteed return.

Storage Hedging: Locking In the Carry

To actually capture carry without speculating on futures direction, you can use a "storage hedge":

üìù Example: Storage Hedge

At harvest (October): You store 10,000 bu. December futures = $4.50, July futures = $4.70 (20¬¢ carry)

Action: SELL December futures at $4.50 (you're now hedged)

Later (December): "Roll" hedge by buying back December at $4.40, selling July at $4.60

In spring (May): Sell cash corn, buy back July futures

Result: You captured the basis improvement from harvest to spring, regardless of where absolute prices went. Your return = basis gain ‚àí storage costs.

This strategy is best executed through your elevator's HTA contract with rolling provisions, rather than trading futures directly.

CH 6 Reading USDA Reports ‚Äî What Moves Markets

USDA reports are the single biggest market movers in agriculture. According to CME Group, "when WASDE reports differ from polling expectation, the market responds: lower-than-expected ending stocks are bullish for futures prices, while larger-than-expected ending stocks are bearish." Understanding which reports matter ‚Äî and what to look for ‚Äî can help you anticipate market reactions and time your marketing decisions.

The Big Five: Reports That Move Markets

WASDE Monthly (around 12th) Supply/demand balance sheets; ending stocks HIGHEST ‚Äî Most important regular report
Prospective Plantings Late March (once/year) Intended planted acres for coming year HIGHEST ‚Äî Sets year's supply expectations
Acreage Late June (once/year) Actual planted acres; updates March intentions HIGH ‚Äî Confirms or surprises from March
Grain Stocks Quarterly (Jan, Mar, Jun, Sep) Actual stocks on-farm and commercial HIGH ‚Äî Reality check on demand estimates
Crop Progress Weekly (Apr-Nov) Planting/harvest progress; crop conditions MODERATE ‚Äî Cumulative impact over season

CH 7 Crop Insurance ‚Äî YP vs RP Explained

Crop insurance is the foundation of farm risk management. According to University of Illinois' farmdoc, Revenue Protection (RP) is used on over 90% of insured corn, soybean, and wheat acres ‚Äî making it the dominant choice for Midwest grain farmers. But is it the right choice for YOUR operation? Understanding the differences between Yield Protection (YP) and Revenue Protection (RP) can save you thousands in premiums ‚Äî or prevent catastrophic losses.

The Two Main Products: YP vs RP

Feature Yield Protection (YP) Revenue Protection (RP)
What it protects Bushels (yield only) Dollars (revenue = yield √ó price)
Price used Projected price only Higher of projected OR harvest price
Pays if... Yield drops below guarantee Revenue drops below guarantee (yield OR price)
Premium cost Lower (10-30% less) Higher
Price protection None Yes ‚Äî guarantee can increase
Best for Tight budgets; price-confident producers Most farmers; forward contractors

How the Guarantees Are Calculated

Yield Protection (YP)

YP Guarantee = APH Yield √ó Coverage Level √ó Projected Price
Example: 180 bu/ac APH √ó 80% √ó $4.66 = $671.04/acre guarantee

With YP, your indemnity is calculated by comparing actual yield to your guaranteed yield, then multiplying the shortfall by the projected price (set in February). YP does NOT use the harvest price.

Revenue Protection (RP)

RP Guarantee = APH Yield √ó Coverage Level √ó MAX(Projected Price, Harvest Price)
Example: If harvest price rises to $5.20, guarantee becomes 180 √ó 80% √ó $5.20 = $748.80/acre

With RP, your guarantee can increase if harvest prices are higher than projected prices. This is the key advantage ‚Äî and why RP is so popular among farmers who forward contract.

Side-by-Side Comparison: 3 Scenarios

Let's compare YP and RP under different harvest outcomes. Assumptions: APH = 180 bu/ac, Coverage = 80%, Projected Price = $4.50

üìù Scenario 1: Low Yield, Price Unchanged

Actual yield: 130 bu/ac | Harvest price: $4.50

YP: Guarantee = 144 bu √ó $4.50 = $648. Actual = 130 √ó $4.50 = $585. Loss = $63/acre
RP: Same calculation = $63/acre

Result: Same payment ‚Äî both products perform identically when prices don't change.

üìù Scenario 2: Low Yield, Price Drops

Actual yield: 130 bu/ac | Harvest price: $3.80

YP: Guarantee = 144 bu √ó $4.50 = $648. Actual = 130 √ó $4.50 = $585. Loss = $63/acre
RP: Guarantee = 144 bu √ó $4.50 = $648. Actual = 130 √ó $3.80 = $494. Loss = $154/acre

Result: RP pays $91/acre MORE because it protects against both the yield loss AND the price decline.

üìù Scenario 3: Low Yield, Price Rises

Actual yield: 130 bu/ac | Harvest price: $5.50

YP: Guarantee = 144 bu √ó $4.50 = $648. Actual = 130 √ó $4.50 = $585. Loss = $63/acre
RP: Guarantee = 144 bu √ó $5.50 = $792. Actual = 130 √ó $5.50 = $715. Loss = $77/acre

Result: RP pays $14/acre MORE because the guarantee increased with the higher harvest price.

‚úÖ Why RP Matters for Forward Contractors

If you forward contract grain and then have a short crop, you must buy bushels at market price to fulfill your contract. If prices have risen, RP's increased guarantee helps offset that cost. Iowa State Extension calls this "especially useful for producers who normally forward price much of their production before harvest."

What About RP-HPE?

Revenue Protection with Harvest Price Exclusion (RP-HPE) is a middle-ground option:

  • Provides revenue protection (price √ó yield)
  • But the guarantee does NOT increase if harvest price exceeds projected price
  • Premium is lower than RP but higher than YP
  • Best for: Producers who don't forward contract and want revenue protection at lower cost

Coverage Levels: How Much to Buy?

Coverage levels range from 50% to 85% (some areas offer higher). The trade-off is simple: higher coverage = more protection = higher premium.

Coverage Level Premium Subsidy Your Share Typical Use
50% (CAT) 100% $0 + admin fee Minimum protection; FSA requirement
65% 59% 41% Budget-conscious; lower-risk areas
75% 55% 45% Moderate protection
80% 48% 52% Most common choice
85% 38% 62% Maximum individual protection

Enterprise Units (combining all acres of a crop in a county) typically receive higher subsidies and lower premiums than Optional Units (separate coverage per farm/section). Most farmers use enterprise units for cost savings.

Which Should You Choose?

Your Situation Recommendation
Forward contract significant production before harvest RP ‚Äî Need harvest price protection
Carry operating debt; need maximum protection RP at 80-85%
Strong balance sheet; confident in marketing YP or RP-HPE ‚Äî Save on premium
New/beginning farmer RP ‚Äî Use extra 10% subsidy discount
Livestock feeder (may need to buy grain) RP ‚Äî Covers replacement cost in short crop year
üí° Bottom Line

For most Upper Midwest grain farmers, Revenue Protection at 80% coverage with enterprise units is the standard recommendation. The extra premium over YP (typically 10-15%) buys critical price protection that can be worth tens of thousands of dollars in a year when both yields and prices drop ‚Äî the "double-whammy" scenario that has bankrupted many farms.

CH 8 Hedging Strategies ‚Äî Protecting Your Price

Hedging is about reducing risk, not maximizing profit. While speculators try to predict market direction, hedgers use futures and options to lock in acceptable prices and reduce uncertainty. For farmers, the goal isn't to "beat the market" ‚Äî it's to ensure profitability regardless of where prices go.

The Three Hedging Tools

üìâ Short Futures Hedge

Sell futures contracts to lock in a price. Obligates delivery or offset. Full downside protection, but gives up all upside.

Price Floor: YES Price Ceiling: YES
üõ°Ô∏è Put Options

Buy the right (not obligation) to sell at strike price. Pay premium upfront. Full downside protection AND keep upside potential.

Price Floor: YES Upside: UNLIMITED
üîÑ Fence/Collar

Buy put + sell call. Put provides floor; call premium offsets put cost. Limited upside, but lower net cost than puts alone.

Price Floor: YES Price Ceiling: YES (chosen)

Strategy #1: Short Futures Hedge

The most straightforward hedge: sell futures contracts equal to your expected production. This locks in the futures price component.

üìù Example: Short Hedge on 25,000 Bushels

May: December corn futures = $4.80. You're comfortable with this price.

Action: SELL 5 December corn contracts (5 √ó 5,000 = 25,000 bu) at $4.80

October: December futures have fallen to $4.20. Local cash bid = $3.95 (‚àí25¬¢ basis)

Close hedge: BUY 5 December contracts at $4.20

Futures gain: ($4.80 ‚àí $4.20) √ó 25,000 = +$15,000
Cash sale: 25,000 √ó $3.95 = $98,750
Total received: $98,750 + $15,000 = $113,750
Effective price: $113,750 √∑ 25,000 = $4.55/bu

The hedge added 60¬¢/bu to your realized price vs. selling unhedged at $3.95.

‚ö†Ô∏è Margin Risk

If prices RISE after you sell futures, you'll face margin calls requiring immediate cash deposits ‚Äî even though your physical crop is worth more. A $1.00/bu move against 5 contracts = $25,000 margin call. Many farmers prefer elevator contracts (forward, HTA) to avoid this cash flow stress.

Strategy #2: Put Options ‚Äî Floor with Upside

Put options give you the RIGHT (not obligation) to sell futures at a specific "strike price." You pay a premium upfront, but keep unlimited upside potential.

Minimum Price = Strike Price ‚àí Premium ‚àí Expected Basis
Example: $4.50 strike ‚àí 25¬¢ premium ‚àí 30¬¢ basis = $3.95/bu floor
üìù Example: Put Option Protection

May: December corn futures = $4.80. You buy a $4.50 put option for 25¬¢/bu premium.

Scenario A ‚Äî Prices fall to $4.00:

Put value: $4.50 ‚àí $4.00 = $0.50 intrinsic value
Net after premium: $0.50 ‚àí $0.25 = $0.25 gain
Cash price: $3.75 (‚àí25¬¢ basis) + $0.25 put gain = $4.00/bu effective

Scenario B ‚Äî Prices rise to $5.50:

Put expires worthless (you wouldn't exercise right to sell at $4.50 when market is $5.50)
Cash price: $5.25 (‚àí25¬¢ basis) ‚àí $0.25 premium = $5.00/bu effective

The put cost you 25¬¢ in premium, but you kept the upside when prices rallied.

Strategy #3: Fence (Collar)

A fence combines buying a put (floor) with selling a call (ceiling). The call premium received offsets part or all of the put premium cost.

üìù Example: Zero-Cost Fence

May: December corn = $4.80

Buy $4.50 put for 25¬¢ (pays if prices fall below $4.50)

Sell $5.20 call for 25¬¢ (obligates you if prices exceed $5.20)

Net premium: $0 (25¬¢ paid ‚àí 25¬¢ received)

Price floor: $4.50 ‚àí basis = ~$4.20-4.25/bu minimum
Price ceiling: $5.20 ‚àí basis = ~$4.90-4.95/bu maximum

You've locked in a $4.20-4.95 range with zero premium cost. You give up gains above $5.20 but are protected below $4.50.

Choosing a Strategy

Your Situation Recommended Strategy
Want complete price certainty; OK giving up upside Short futures or forward contract
Want protection but expect prices may rally Put options
Want protection but can't afford put premium Fence (sell call to pay for put)
Already forward contracted; need production protection Call options (cover short crop scenario)
Want to avoid margin calls Elevator contracts (HTA, forward, basis)

Important Hedging Rules

  1. Never hedge 100% of expected production: Yield variability means you may not have the bushels to deliver. Hedge 50-70% maximum pre-harvest.
  2. Match contract months: Use December corn for fall-harvested corn; July for old-crop sales. Mismatched months create spread risk.
  3. Understand basis: Hedging locks in futures, not cash price. Your final price = futures price ¬± basis. (See Chapter 1)
  4. Have a plan for margin: If using futures directly, ensure you have credit lines or cash reserves to meet margin calls.
  5. Consider crop insurance interaction: RP's harvest price protection complements hedging by covering production shortfalls at potentially higher prices.
‚úÖ Practical Approach for Most Farmers

Instead of trading futures/options directly, most farmers achieve similar results through elevator contracts: Forward contracts = short hedge; HTA = locks futures, open basis; Basis contracts = locks basis, open futures; Minimum price contracts = put option equivalent. These tools let you hedge without margin accounts, daily settlements, or broker relationships.

Sources: CME Group, "Hedging with Agricultural Futures"; Iowa State Extension, "Introduction to Hedging Agricultural Commodities"; University of Illinois Extension, "Options Strategies for Grain Marketing."

CH 9 Break-Even Analysis ‚Äî Know Your Numbers

You can't market grain profitably if you don't know your costs. Break-even analysis is the foundation of every marketing decision ‚Äî it tells you the minimum price you need to cover costs and stay in business. According to Purdue University's 2025 Crop Cost & Return Guide, understanding your true cost of production is "critical for making informed marketing and financial decisions."

The Break-Even Formula

Break-Even Price = Total Cost per Acre √∑ Expected Yield
Example: $850/acre √∑ 190 bu/acre = $4.47/bu break-even

This simple formula hides significant complexity. The challenge is accurately calculating "Total Cost per Acre" ‚Äî which includes costs many farmers overlook.

Cost Categories: Don't Miss Any

Category Components Typical Range (Corn)
Seed Seed cost, treatment, replant reserve $110-140/acre
Fertilizer N, P, K, lime, micronutrients, application $150-220/acre
Crop Protection Herbicides, insecticides, fungicides, application $50-90/acre
Crop Insurance Premium (your share after subsidy) $20-45/acre
Drying & Storage Propane/electric, handling, shrink $25-50/acre
Interest Operating loan interest on inputs $15-35/acre
Machinery Fuel, repairs, depreciation $80-130/acre
Labor Hired + operator labor value $25-50/acre
Land Cash rent OR ownership cost $150-350/acre
Overhead Utilities, insurance, professional fees, misc $20-40/acre

Variable vs. Fixed Costs

Understanding the difference matters for short-term vs. long-term decisions:

per acre ‚Äî changes with production

  • Variable costs: Seed, fertilizer, chemicals, fuel, drying ‚Äî costs that wouldn't occur if you didn't plant
  • Fixed costs: Land rent, machinery depreciation, insurance, labor ‚Äî costs incurred whether you plant or not

Short-term decision: If prices only cover variable costs, you may still plant (better to lose less than lose all fixed costs). Long-term: Must cover ALL costs to remain viable.

Using Break-Even in Marketing

Once you know your break-even, you can make informed pricing decisions:

Price vs. Break-Even Action
Price 15%+ above break-even Strong profit opportunity ‚Äî consider aggressive forward pricing (40-60%)
Price 5-15% above break-even Good return ‚Äî scale-up pricing as targets hit (20-40%)
Price near break-even (¬±5%) Marginal ‚Äî price minimally; wait for better opportunities
Price below break-even Loss territory ‚Äî use puts for floor; don't lock in losses with forwards

Common Mistakes in Break-Even Analysis

  1. Forgetting land cost: Whether you rent or own, land has a cost. Owners should use opportunity cost (what you could rent it for) or actual ownership costs.
  2. Ignoring operator labor: Your time has value. If you wouldn't work for free elsewhere, don't work for free on your farm.
  3. Using last year's costs: Input prices change. Recalculate break-even each year with current costs.
  4. Overly optimistic yields: Use realistic APH or 5-year average, not your best year. Consider using 90% of APH for conservative planning.
  5. Missing interest costs: At 8-10% operating loan rates, interest on inputs can add 15-35¬¢/bu to break-even.
‚úÖ Best Practice: Calculate Three Break-Evens

Optimistic: APH + 10% yield, covers variable costs only = "worst case still-plant" threshold
Expected: APH yield, covers all costs = primary marketing target
Conservative: APH - 10% yield, covers all costs = stress-test for aggressive pricing

CH 10 Growing Degree Units ‚Äî Tracking Crop Progress

Corn development is driven by heat, not calendar days. Growing Degree Units (GDUs), also called Growing Degree Days (GDDs), measure accumulated heat that drives crop growth. Understanding GDUs helps you predict growth stages, time inputs, and assess crop progress ‚Äî critical knowledge for both production and marketing decisions.

The GDU Formula

Daily GDU = [(High + Low) √∑ 2] ‚àí 50
With limits: High capped at 86¬∞F, Low floored at 50¬∞F

The formula uses 50¬∞F as the "base temperature" because corn growth essentially stops below this threshold. The 86¬∞F cap reflects research showing that temperatures above 86¬∞F don't accelerate corn growth ‚Äî they may actually stress the plant.

üìù Example: Daily GDU Calculation

Day 1: High = 78¬∞F, Low = 56¬∞F

GDU = [(78 + 56) √∑ 2] ‚àí 50 = 67 ‚àí 50 = 17 GDUs

Day 2: High = 92¬∞F (cap at 86), Low = 68¬∞F

GDU = [(86 + 68) √∑ 2] ‚àí 50 = 77 ‚àí 50 = 27 GDUs

Day 3: High = 62¬∞F, Low = 48¬∞F (floor at 50)

GDU = [(62 + 50) √∑ 2] ‚àí 50 = 56 ‚àí 50 = 6 GDUs

3-Day Total: 17 + 27 + 6 = 50 GDUs accumulated

Corn Growth Stages by GDU

Hybrid seed companies rate corn maturities by "Relative Maturity" (RM), which correlates to GDUs needed to reach black layer (physiological maturity). A typical relationship:

Growth Stage GDUs from Planting What's Happening
VE (Emergence) 100-120 Coleoptile breaks soil surface
V6 (6-leaf) 475-500 Growing point above ground; ear shoots initiated
V12 (12-leaf) 870-920 Rapid growth; kernel rows determined
VT (Tassel) 1,135-1,200 Tassel fully emerged; 2-3 days before silk
R1 (Silking) 1,200-1,350 Pollination window ‚Äî CRITICAL 2-week period
R2 (Blister) 1,400-1,500 Kernels white, watery; 85% moisture
R3 (Milk) 1,600-1,700 Kernels yellow, milky fluid; 80% moisture
R4 (Dough) 1,900-2,000 Starch accumulating; 70% moisture
R5 (Dent) 2,200-2,400 Kernel denting; milk line visible
R6 (Black Layer) 2,500-2,800 Physiological maturity; ~30% moisture

Note: GDU requirements vary by hybrid. A 95-day hybrid needs approximately 2,500 GDUs to black layer; a 110-day hybrid needs approximately 2,800 GDUs.

Why GDUs Matter for Marketing

Tracking GDUs helps you make better marketing decisions:

  • Yield potential assessment: If GDU accumulation is behind normal at silking, yield potential may be reduced ‚Äî adjust forward sales accordingly
  • Harvest timing: Predict when your crop will reach black layer and estimate harvest moisture ‚Äî affects drying costs in break-even
  • Frost risk: Calculate GDUs remaining to maturity vs. GDUs expected before first frost ‚Äî critical for late-planted crops
  • Market timing: Late-maturing crops may hit harvest during different basis/price windows than early crops
üìù Example: Frost Risk Assessment

Situation: August 15, your 105-day corn (needs 2,650 GDUs) has accumulated 1,800 GDUs.

GDUs needed: 2,650 ‚àí 1,800 = 850 GDUs remaining

Your area averages: 18 GDUs/day in late August, 14 GDUs/day in September

Days to maturity: ~50-55 days ‚Üí Black layer around October 5-10

Average first frost: October 10

Assessment: Marginal ‚Äî crop should reach maturity, but a cold September could delay black layer past frost. Consider: 1) insurance implications, 2) whether to price more now vs. waiting, 3) harvest logistics for potentially wetter corn.
