üìä Understanding Basis

Basis is the difference between your local cash price and the futures price. It's calculated as: Cash Price - Futures Price = Basis

Why Basis Matters

Basis reflects local supply and demand conditions. Understanding basis patterns helps you make better marketing decisions by separating the "local" component from the "global" futures price.

Factors Affecting Basis

  • Transportation costs: Distance to terminal markets
  • Local supply/demand: Nearby ethanol plants, livestock feeders
  • Storage availability: Commercial and on-farm capacity
  • Quality premiums/discounts: Moisture, test weight, damage
  • Time of year: Harvest pressure vs. tight supplies

Using Basis in Marketing

Track your local basis history to identify patterns. A "stronger" (less negative) basis means better local prices relative to futures. Consider basis contracts when basis is historically strong but you're uncertain about futures direction.

üí∞ Grain Marketing Strategies

Successful grain marketing involves spreading sales across time and using multiple pricing tools. No single strategy works in all market conditions.

Common Strategies

  • Scale-up selling: Sell portions as prices rise to target levels
  • Seasonal patterns: Consider typical harvest lows and summer highs
  • Cost-based targets: Know your break-even and set profitable targets
  • Basis contracts: Lock in strong basis, leave futures open
  • Hedge-to-Arrive: Lock in futures, leave basis open

Risk Management Tips

Never sell 100% of expected production before harvest. Leave flexibility for yield surprises. Consider crop insurance as part of your overall risk management plan.

üìà Futures Contracts 101

Futures contracts are standardized agreements to buy or sell a commodity at a specific price on a future date. They're used for price discovery and risk management.

Contract Specifications

  • Corn (ZC): 5,000 bushels, #2 Yellow
  • Soybeans (ZS): 5,000 bushels, #2 Yellow
  • Wheat (ZW): 5,000 bushels, #2 Soft Red Winter

Contract Months

Corn & Soybeans: March, May, July, September, December
Wheat: March, May, July, September, December

How Farmers Use Futures

Farmers typically use futures to hedge (reduce risk), not speculate. A short hedge involves selling futures to protect against price declines. When you eventually sell your physical grain, you buy back the futures position.
