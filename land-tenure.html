Who owns the ground?

Everybody argues about who's buying farmland. The census answers a quieter question first: how much of it is farmed by someone who doesn't own it at all. 39.3% of U.S. farmland — 346 million of 880 million acres — is rented from someone else (2022 census) · 691 of 3,020 counties are majority-rented · Illinois leads the Corn Belt at 55.8% · data refreshed 2026-07-18

Questions people actually ask

What share of U.S. farmland is rented?
In the 2022 Census of Agriculture, farms reported renting 346 million of the 880 million acres they operate — 39.3%. In 1997 it was 40.3%. The share is remarkably stable nationally, but it varies enormously by county: 691 counties are majority-rented.
Which states have the most rented farmland?
Illinois (55.8%), Louisiana (55.3%), Kansas (54.3%), North Dakota (53.9%) and Indiana (52.2%) lead among major farm states — in each, most of the farmland is worked by someone who doesn't own it. Iowa sits at 49.8%. New England and Arizona are the opposite extreme, under 16%.
Where does this data come from?
The USDA Census of Agriculture, taken every five years (1997, 2002, 2007, 2012, 2017, 2022). Farms report acres owned and acres rented from others. This page divides rented-from-others by total land in farms. No survey sampling, no modeling — it's the census.
Does rented farmland mean absentee landlords?
Not necessarily. Rented ground includes land owned by retired farmers, widows and heirs still in the county, family members, and local investors — as well as out-of-area owners. The census counts the tenure arrangement, not where the landlord sleeps. What it does tell you: who carries the operating risk (the renter) and who collects a fixed rent (the owner).
Why does the rented share matter for cash rent?
The bigger the rented share, the more of a county's economics run through the rental market — and the more the county cash rent number is the number. That's why this page links straight to the county rent map and the printable lease.
